# Sports Analytics Specialist — Example Output Shapes

Use these patterns to keep sports analysis contextual, tactically meaningful, and honest about uncertainty.

## Example 1 — Player comparison

**Input:**
Compare two football strikers for recruitment using xG, shot profile, pressing, and role context rather than goals alone.

**Good output shape:**
- recruitment objective and role definition
- comparison table with context notes
- what the metrics suggest tactically
- uncertainty, league-adjustment, and sample caveats
- recommendation and what to scout next

## Example 2 — Team-offense diagnosis

**Input:**
Explain why a basketball team looks weak in raw points but stronger in efficiency terms.

**Good output shape:**
- pace and possession context
- key metrics and what they capture
- tactical explanation for the discrepancy
- what is sustainable versus likely noise
- coaching or media-facing takeaway

## Example 3 — Matchup brief

**Input:**
Build a pre-match brief for a hockey team facing an opponent with strong transition play and a weak penalty kill.

**Good output shape:**
- game context and opponent profile
- strengths, vulnerabilities, and state-specific notes
- metrics translated into tactical consequences
- caveats about lineup or recent-form noise
- priority recommendations

## Example 4 — Media note for non-analysts

**Input:**
Turn advanced baseball stats into a clear note for broadcast talent.

**Good output shape:**
- simple framing of the story
- brief definitions of useful metrics
- what the numbers mean in baseball terms
- one or two caution flags against overstatement
- talkable lines for air

## Example 5 — Trend vs noise check

**Input:**
A player’s last five games look elite. Is this a real jump or a small-sample spike?

**Good output shape:**
- timeframe and baseline comparison
- changes in role, usage, opponent mix, or shooting luck
- which indicators deserve attention
- what is too early to claim
- monitoring plan for the next sample window
