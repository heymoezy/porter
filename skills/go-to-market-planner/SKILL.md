# Go-to-Market Planner

Use this skill to turn an offer into a believable path from market attention to revenue.

## Start from market truth

Gather the minimum facts that make GTM work real:
- product or offer
- target buyer, user, champion, and economic decision-maker
- pain severity and trigger event
- status-quo alternative and competitor set
- differentiated promise and proof points
- pricing, packaging, sales motion, and buyer friction
- launch objective: awareness, pipeline, activation, adoption, expansion, or category entry
- budget, timing, channel constraints, and internal dependencies

If those inputs are weak, say so early. GTM fails when the plan outruns the evidence.

## Core workflow

1. **Choose the wedge**
   - Pick the narrowest segment or use case where value is most obvious.
   - Prefer a credible beachhead over a broad market fantasy.
2. **Define positioning before channel plans**
   - For whom
   - For what problem or moment
   - Against what alternative
   - Why this offer wins
   - What proof makes the claim believable
3. **Map the buying journey**
   - How the market discovers the offer
   - What message angle fits each touchpoint
   - What conversion step comes next
   - Where sales, product, or support must take over
4. **Build the launch system**
   - phases
   - owners
   - deliverables
   - dependencies
   - metrics
5. **Pressure-test assumptions**
   - Which beliefs are unproven?
   - Which bottlenecks could kill the launch?
   - What signals mean stay the course vs change direction?

## What good output looks like

Return practical deliverables such as:
- ICP and segment choice with rationale
- positioning statement and message hierarchy
- channel plan tied to buyer behavior
- 30/60/90-day or phased launch plan
- readiness gaps and risk register
- validation experiments and launch metrics

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- narrow initial focus
- proof-backed positioning
- channels justified by buyer behavior
- explicit assumptions and tradeoffs
- measurable commercial outcomes

Avoid:
- generic channel checklists
- ICPs that describe everyone
- messaging that sounds interchangeable with competitors
- launch plans that ignore enablement, support, or product readiness
- vanity metrics disconnected from pipeline, activation, or revenue

## Boundary calls

Use adjacent skills instead when needed:
- **competitive-analyst** for deeper competitor comparison work
- **pricing-strategist** when price/packaging is the main design problem
- **copywriter** or **content-writer** for final asset drafting
- **product-manager** for roadmap and product-prioritization decisions

## Final check

Before finishing, verify:
- the target segment is concrete
- the wedge is narrow enough to win
- the positioning clearly beats the status quo
- channels match how the buyer actually buys
- assumptions, risks, and metrics are explicit
