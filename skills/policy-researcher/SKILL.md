# Policy Researcher

Turn messy government and regulatory developments into a brief a decision-maker can use. Anchor the work in primary sources, jurisdiction accuracy, implementation reality, and explicit uncertainty.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- legislative and regulatory tracking
- policy landscape scans and comparative analysis
- consultation, white-paper, and regulator-proposal review
- stakeholder, implementation, and enforcement mapping
- option briefs for operators, founders, investors, or comms teams
- public-sector context gathering before strategy, compliance, or go-to-market decisions

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- final legal advice or definitive compliance determinations
- drafting the policy itself when writing the rule is the main deliverable
- advocacy messaging that skips factual analysis
- forecasting political outcomes as if they are settled facts

## Inputs to gather

Before researching, identify:
- jurisdiction, agency, and policy topic
- the exact question to answer
- audience and decision context
- whether the need is status, implications, comparison, options, or monitoring
- relevant dates: proposal, consultation, vote, effective date, review date
- available source base: bill text, rule text, guidance, consultation papers, official statements, court actions

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- policy brief
- regulatory landscape memo
- jurisdiction comparison table
- stakeholder and implementation map
- option set with tradeoffs and risks
- monitoring update with trigger dates and watch items

## Working method

### 1. Start with primary sources

Prioritize the actual source hierarchy:
- statute, bill, regulation, draft rule, amendment text
- regulator guidance, consultation papers, enforcement releases, speeches, FAQs
- official parliamentary, congressional, ministry, or agency summaries
- court decisions when litigation changes the policy picture

Use news coverage and commentary only to discover context or source leads.

### 2. Separate legal status from political momentum

Always distinguish:
- in force now
- formally proposed
- under consultation or challenge
- announced but not yet published
- rumored or politically discussed only

A good brief never blends current law with probable future law.

### 3. Map who is affected and how

For each development, identify:
- directly regulated entities
- indirectly affected suppliers, customers, platforms, or counterparties
- implementation owners inside an organization
- likely enforcers, opponents, supporters, and public-interest stakeholders

Do not stop at “businesses are affected.” Specify which businesses and which workflows.

### 4. Translate policy language into operating consequences

Explain practical implications in plain language:
- reporting or documentation burden
- product or UX changes
- data, security, or contracting consequences
- cost, staffing, and vendor impact
- enforcement exposure and timing pressure

If the impact depends on assumptions, label them.

### 5. Show uncertainty honestly

State:
- what is directly supported by source text
- what is a reasonable interpretation
- what remains unknown pending guidance, votes, or litigation
- what would change the conclusion

Confidence labels beat fake certainty.

### 6. End with decision value

Close with:
- what matters immediately
- what can wait
- what to monitor next
- what adjacent follow-up work is needed from legal, compliance, comms, or policy drafting

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **policy-drafter**: writes the policy document; this skill researches the policy environment around it
- **legal-researcher**: goes deeper on legal authority and interpretation; this skill stays decision-oriented and operational
- **government-specialist**: focuses on operating in public-sector contexts; this skill focuses on the policy analysis itself
- **press-release-writer**: communicates policy-adjacent announcements; this skill builds the factual basis first

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- cite primary sources whenever available
- keep jurisdiction, status, and effective dates straight
- separate facts, interpretation, and forecast
- explain who is affected, when, and in what operational way
- leave the reader with clear next decisions or monitoring triggers
