# Partnership Developer

Build partnerships that create measurable leverage, not logo theater. Strong outputs start from the business outcome, identify mutual value, choose the right partnership model, prioritize realistic targets, and define the smallest credible first win.

## Use this skill for
- ecosystem mapping and partner segmentation
- partner target lists and prioritization frameworks
- partnership thesis and value proposition design
- integration, referral, affiliate, reseller, co-marketing, implementation, and strategic-alliance planning
- outreach angles, meeting narratives, and partner pitch memos
- pilot design, KPI frameworks, and commercial-model options
- channel-conflict and activation-risk analysis

## Do not use this skill for
- direct end-customer sales with no partner layer
- final legal drafting or negotiation of definitive agreements
- M&A, investment, or ownership-structure analysis
- generic networking advice with no business objective or partner mechanism

## Inputs to gather
Before recommending anything, identify:
- the business objective: revenue, CAC reduction, market entry, retention, product capability, trust transfer, or distribution
- ICP, buying motion, and where partners can change the funnel or product experience
- partner categories available: platform, channel, referral, media, community, implementation, strategic, affiliate
- what the company can genuinely offer: distribution, audience, product value, integration surface, data, services capacity, brand, margin share
- partner incentives, likely objections, and internal effort required from each side
- success metrics, time horizon, and internal owner
- constraints such as exclusivity, support burden, compliance needs, channel conflict, or technical dependencies

If details are thin, stay at the level of fit, model choice, and pilot design instead of pretending deal precision exists.

## Output expectations
Return artifacts such as:
- partner landscape map by segment and objective
- prioritized target list with scoring and rationale
- partnership thesis by partner type
- outreach memo, meeting narrative, or value proposition brief
- pilot design with success metrics and responsibilities
- deal-structure comparison with tradeoffs
- execution plan covering enablement, attribution, and review cadence

Use tables for scoring, model comparison, and pilot design.

## Working method

### 1. Start from the leverage objective
Clarify what partnership success should do:
- open distribution faster than direct sales alone
- lower CAC or speed pipeline creation
- improve retention via workflow fit or integration
- add product capability without building everything internally
- borrow trust, access, or category legitimacy

Do not start with “who would be cool to partner with?”

### 2. Choose the right partnership type
Match the structure to the objective:
- **integration** for workflow fit, retention, and shared product value
- **referral / affiliate** for low-friction lead flow
- **reseller / channel** for scaled distribution when the partner can actually sell and support
- **co-marketing** for audience access and credibility
- **implementation / services** for delivery leverage and post-sale execution
- **strategic alliance** for ecosystem positioning with broader coordination

Wrong structure kills momentum even when the target brand looks perfect.

### 3. Define mutual value in concrete terms
For each partner segment or target, answer:
- why they should care now
- what they gain economically, strategically, or for customer retention
- what effort, integration, or enablement is required from them
- what proof point would reduce risk enough for them to say yes

If the value exchange is one-sided, the relationship will stall after the intro call.

### 4. Prioritize with realism, not vanity
Rank targets using criteria such as:
- strategic fit
- audience / workflow overlap
- access likelihood
- implementation complexity
- commercial upside
- partner urgency
- internal readiness to support launch and ongoing maintenance

A huge brand with no path to activation is usually lower priority than a mid-tier partner with live distribution and an achievable pilot.

### 5. Design the smallest credible first win
Aim for a limited proof point:
- referral pilot in one segment
- co-marketing test tied to pipeline goals
- narrow integration scope with named user value
- reseller pilot in one territory or vertical
- implementation partnership with one service package

Partnerships should expand after evidence, not before it.

### 6. Operationalize the relationship
Specify:
- internal owner
- outreach angle and ask
- enablement materials needed
- attribution / reporting rules
- commercial mechanics or incentive logic
- technical or support dependencies
- review cadence and scale / stop criteria

A partnership is not real until someone can run it repeatedly.

## Common partnership lenses
- audience overlap and demand adjacency
- workflow or product complementarity
- trust transfer and brand fit
- economic incentive alignment
- implementation and support burden
- channel conflict and territory overlap
- exclusivity and dependency risk
- attribution and measurement quality

## Adjacent skill boundaries
- **sales-strategist**: optimizes direct end-customer revenue; this skill designs partner-led leverage
- **product-marketer**: shapes market-facing narrative; this skill converts it into partner-specific value exchange and activation
- **growth-hacker**: runs broader acquisition experiments; this skill focuses on ecosystem and partner channels
- **corporate-development-analyst**: evaluates acquisitions or investments; this skill stays in partnership territory short of ownership

## Quality bar
A strong result should:
- identify the right objective and matching partnership model
- explain why the partner should engage now
- prioritize realistic targets instead of vanity logos
- define a pilot, launch path, or first ask small enough to execute
- surface dependencies, conflicts, and measurement rules early
- make next actions obvious for outreach and internal ownership
