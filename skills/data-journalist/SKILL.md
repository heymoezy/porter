# Data Journalist

Use data to uncover a story worth telling, then show the reader how you know.

This skill is for editorial work built on evidence: finding the real angle, pressure-testing whether the numbers support it, and writing with enough transparency that the argument survives scrutiny.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- data-driven story angle generation
- findings briefs and explanatory article structures
- chart-led narrative sequencing
- methodology notes and sourcing plans
- claim-evidence-caveat ledgers
- headline and framing review for evidentiary accuracy
- public-report and newsroom-style synthesis of quantitative findings

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- a compelling but defensible story built from data
- an answer to whether the dataset really supports a headline or narrative
- a methodology section that a skeptical reader could follow
- a chart sequence that clarifies rather than sensationalizes
- public-facing analysis that translates numbers for non-specialists without dumbing them down

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- basic dashboard interpretation with no editorial output
- policy drafting or governance controls
- academic literature review as the primary task
- opinion writing that is not evidence-led
- causal claims that the available data cannot support

## Inputs to gather

Before shaping the story, identify:
- the core question and why it matters now
- who the audience is and what level of statistical literacy to assume
- the data source, collector, timeframe, geography, and unit of analysis
- known gaps, missing populations, proxy fields, or sampling constraints
- what comparisons, baselines, or denominators are required
- what other reporting or corroborating sources are available
- where interpretation could outrun the evidence
- what ethical sensitivities or harm risks exist

If the source trail is weak, the story angle should weaken too.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- ranked story angles
- findings brief
- article or explainer outline
- claim-evidence-caveat ledger
- methodology note
- chart narrative sequence
- evidence-backed headline options

Strong output should feel reportable, publishable, and auditable.

## Working method

### 1. Find the real angle

Ask:
- what is new, surprising, consequential, or underreported
- who is affected and why readers should care now
- whether the strongest angle comes from trend, comparison, disparity, concentration, outlier, or failure of expectation
- whether the proposed angle is actually supported, not just emotionally attractive

Do not let the chart choose the story by accident.

### 2. Audit the evidence before writing the thesis

Check:
- source credibility and collection method
- coverage and representativeness
- definitions and coding choices
- timeframe consistency
- whether key comparisons need adjustment for population, price, seasonality, or category mix
- what the data cannot show

If the evidence is shaky, say so early.

### 3. Build a claim ladder

For each major claim, note:
- the exact supporting data
- what corroboration would strengthen it
- the strongest credible alternative explanation
- caveats, uncertainty, and limits
- confidence level in plain language

Every strong story needs a paper trail.

### 4. Write for clarity without laundering uncertainty

Prefer:
- a sharp nut graph or thesis sentence
- concrete comparisons with denominators and baselines
- plain-language method explanation
- chart captions that do analytical work
- confidence-calibrated verbs like shows, suggests, indicates, or cannot establish

Precision is part of the style.

### 5. Treat visuals as reporting, not decoration

For each chart, define:
- the question it answers
- the comparison or trend it makes visible
- the annotation or context needed to prevent misreading
- the caveat that belongs near the visual, not buried later

A misleading chart can ruin a good investigation.

### 6. Separate findings from interpretation

Mark clearly:
- what is directly observed
- what is inferred
- what still needs reporting, expert comment, or additional verification
- what should be excluded because it is flashy but unsupported

The honest gap is part of the story.

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **data-analyst**: interprets data for internal decision-making; this skill shapes public-facing or editorial narratives from evidence
- **research-analyst**: may synthesize findings for strategic use; this skill is tuned for journalism-style framing, sourcing, and publishable clarity
- **content-writer**: drafts general prose; this skill imposes stronger claim-evidence-method discipline
- **policy-researcher**: focuses on policy implications and source synthesis; this skill focuses on story angle, evidence audit, and narrative integrity
- **dashboard-designer**: designs interactive analytical views; this skill uses charts as part of an explanatory story

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- identify a story that matters and is actually supportable
- make methodology and source quality legible to a skeptical reader
- link claims, evidence, and caveats tightly
- include denominators, baselines, and time context where needed
- avoid turning ambiguity into fake certainty or clickbait framing
