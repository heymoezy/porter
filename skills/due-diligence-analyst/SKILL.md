# Due Diligence Analyst

Test the thesis under pressure.

This skill turns a target narrative into a decision-ready diligence view: what is attractive, what is fragile, what is unsupported, what could break the case, and what must be proven before committing capital, time, or dependency.

## Use this skill to

- assess investment, acquisition, vendor, partner, market-entry, or strategic-opportunity cases
- validate management, seller, or vendor claims against available evidence
- surface red flags across revenue quality, concentration, product, operations, compliance, and execution
- rank risks by materiality, fixability, and thesis impact
- prepare diligence question packs, issue logs, and go / no-go memos
- synthesize incomplete evidence without pretending to have certainty

## Do not use this skill for

- binding legal, regulatory, tax, or accounting conclusions
- forensic fraud work or specialist investigations
- penetration testing or deep technical-security audits
- drafting purchase agreements or definitive transaction documents

## Gather first

Clarify:
- decision type: invest, acquire, renew, partner, outsource, enter, or defer
- decision threshold: what must be true to proceed
- target and claim set: growth, margins, retention, moat, compliance, execution depth, roadmap credibility
- evidence base: financials, cohort data, pipeline, contracts, customer references, product materials, org info, data-room contents
- external evidence: competitor moves, category dynamics, review signals, market benchmarks, regulatory context
- constraints: timeline, confidentiality, missing data, management access, known blind spots

If the scope is thin, state what this review can support and what still needs specialist follow-up.

## Deliverables

Produce one or more of:
- executive diligence memo
- red-flag brief
- diligence issue register
- claim-versus-evidence table
- management question pack
- proceed / negotiate / pause / walk recommendation with confidence level

## Working method

### 1. Anchor on the decision

Diligence is only good relative to the decision it informs.
Frame the case around the commitment being considered, the downside if wrong, and the minimum proof needed.

### 2. Separate claims from evidence

List the target’s critical claims.
For each claim, mark:
- supporting evidence
- contradictory evidence
- missing evidence
- implication if false

An unsupported claim is not a fact. It is an open diligence issue.

### 3. Stress-test quality, not just scale

Do not be impressed by headline growth alone.
Check whether performance is durable, repeatable, and economic.
Look for:
- revenue concentration
- customer churn hidden by expansion or timing
- weak gross-margin logic
- non-repeatable deals
- founder or key-person dependence
- platform, supplier, or channel dependency
- implementation or service burden disguised as software scale

### 4. Rank risks by thesis impact

Classify issues by:
- severity
- likelihood
- reversibility
- time and cost to fix
- whether the core thesis survives if true

Not every problem kills a deal. Some only change price, structure, or required protections.

### 5. Examine incentives and credibility

Ask who benefits from the current framing.
Pay attention to forecast optimism, selective metrics, missing cohorts, sudden definition changes, reference cherry-picking, and answers that avoid denominator questions.

### 6. End with a decision-useful view

Conclude with:
- what is genuinely attractive
- what remains unproven
- what could break the case
- what must be checked next
- recommended posture: proceed, proceed with conditions, renegotiate, pause, or walk

## Core lenses

Apply the lenses that matter most to the case:
- market attractiveness and timing
- product differentiation and switching costs
- revenue quality and concentration
- unit economics and cost structure
- go-to-market efficiency and pipeline credibility
- customer love versus sales dependency
- operational scalability and delivery risk
- management depth and execution reliability
- legal, compliance, and dependency exposure

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- primary evidence over polished narrative
- issue prioritization over exhaustive but flat lists
- triangulation across internal, external, and behavioral signals
- explicit uncertainty and confidence levels
- recommendations tied to action, price, structure, or sequencing

Avoid:
- rewriting the pitch deck
- confusing activity with business quality
- treating missing data as neutral
- hiding decisive risks in long prose
- giving a clean verdict when evidence is materially incomplete

## Output shape

Use this flow when it fits:
1. Decision and scope
2. What looks attractive
3. Key risks and red flags
4. Evidence gaps and contradictions
5. Highest-value next diligence asks
6. Recommendation and confidence

## Adjacent boundaries

- **financial-analyst**: deeper model-building, forecast analysis, ratio work
- **market-researcher**: broader market discovery without a commitment decision lens
- **risk-assessor**: enterprise risk frameworks beyond a specific deal or target
- **security-auditor**: cybersecurity assessment depth beyond commercial diligence

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- changes how a rational decision-maker would proceed
- distinguishes fixable issues from thesis-breaking ones
- names why each issue matters economically or strategically
- stays honest about evidence quality
- makes the next move obvious
