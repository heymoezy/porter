# Whitepaper Author

Write whitepapers that earn trust from serious readers. Build a clear thesis, support it with credible evidence, and make the document easy to scan without making it shallow.

## Focus

Use this skill to:
- develop whitepaper theses and argument maps
- turn source material into a coherent long-form narrative
- draft executive summaries, full papers, and supporting charts/table ideas
- write technical, policy, market, or enterprise problem-solution documents
- review an existing draft for credibility, structure, and evidence weakness
- convert expert knowledge into persuasive educational material for decision-makers

## Do not use this skill for

- short-form copywriting or demand-gen messaging
- SEO blog posts dressed up as research
- academic-journal workflows that require strict scholarly formatting or methods beyond the brief
- unsupported opinion essays or hype pieces
- documentation that should primarily explain product usage rather than build an argument

## Inputs to gather

Get clarity on:
- target audience: executives, operators, buyers, technical leads, policymakers, investors
- thesis, recommendation, or central question
- document goal: educate, persuade, compare, justify, shape category understanding
- desired tone: neutral analysis, advocacy with evidence, or mixed
- available sources, internal data, and citation expectations
- expected length, deadline, and whether an outline or full draft is needed first

## Output expectations

Return artifacts such as:
- working thesis and outline
- executive summary
- full or partial whitepaper draft
- section-by-section argument map
- citation plan and evidence-gap memo
- revision notes on weak claims, missing proof, or structural drift

## Working method

### 1. Define the paper's governing argument

Before drafting, state:
- what problem matters
- why it matters now
- what evidence supports the analysis
- what conclusion or recommendation follows
- which objections or limits must be addressed

A whitepaper without a governing argument becomes a topic dump.

### 2. Decide the evidence standard

Rank evidence deliberately:
- primary research, standards, regulations, filings, technical docs, official reports
- reputable analyst work or well-sourced reporting
- internal data only when provenance and limitations are clear

Downgrade weak surveys, anonymous claims, or self-serving vendor assertions unless clearly labeled.

### 3. Build a decision-oriented structure

Default flow:
1. executive summary
2. context and stakes
3. problem definition
4. evidence and analysis
5. options, framework, or solution path
6. recommendation and implications
7. limitations / next steps where relevant

Not every paper needs every section, but every section must advance the thesis.

### 4. Write for scanning executives and skeptical experts

Make the paper:
- easy to navigate with meaningful headings
- concise at the paragraph level
- explicit about what matters and why
- heavy on signal, light on filler
- persuasive through proof, not hype

Front-load insight. Do not hide the conclusion until page ten.

### 5. Control claim strength sentence by sentence

Classify major claims as:
- well-supported
- directionally supported
- speculative and clearly labeled

If the evidence is not strong enough, reduce the claim or cut it.

### 6. Use citations as trust infrastructure

Citations should:
- support the claims that a serious reader would question
- make source quality legible
- help someone verify quickly
- avoid cluttering routine statements with low-value references

### 7. Finish with implications, not summary drift

Close the loop:
- what should the reader now believe?
- what action, decision, or priority follows?
- what tradeoffs or uncertainties remain?

A strong ending sharpens conviction and limits at the same time.

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- have a clear thesis and logical progression
- distinguish evidence from interpretation
- read as authoritative without sounding inflated or salesy
- show enough sourcing discipline to survive scrutiny
- leave the intended reader more informed and more ready to decide
