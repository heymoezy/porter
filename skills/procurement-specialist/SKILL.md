# Procurement Specialist

Buy with discipline. Good procurement work clarifies the need, creates a fair comparison, prices the real operating cost, exposes supplier risk, and leaves an auditable reason for the decision. Lowest quote is not the same as best purchase.

## Focus

Use this skill to:
- turn a vague purchase need into a structured sourcing path
- define requirements, constraints, and evaluation logic
- build RFP/RFQ frameworks and vendor scorecards
- compare suppliers on fit, cost, risk, and implementation burden
- analyze total cost of ownership and renewal exposure
- recommend approval gates, pilots, and pre-signature diligence

Do not use this skill to:
- draft or negotiate legal contract language as the main deliverable
- process invoices or accounts-payable workflows
- manage day-to-day vendor performance after contracting
- make buying recommendations based only on vendor marketing claims

## Inputs to pin down

Clarify as much of this as possible before comparing options:
- business problem and desired outcome
- must-have requirements vs preferences
- budget ceiling, timeline pressure, and deadline non-negotiables
- implementation, migration, training, and change-management burden
- integration, security, privacy, compliance, and data-handling needs
- expected usage volume, growth, and overage exposure
- stakeholders: requester, operator, approver, finance, IT, security, legal, procurement
- whether a pilot, phased rollout, or incumbent comparison is available

## Deliverables this skill should produce

Return outputs such as:
- sourcing strategy memos
- weighted vendor scorecards
- RFP/RFQ structures and question sets
- total-cost-of-ownership comparisons
- go / no-go / conditional-go recommendations
- approval-path and diligence checklists before signature

## Working method

### 1. Clarify the need before you compare suppliers

Separate:
- business outcome
- operational requirements
- technical constraints
- regulatory or risk constraints
- real must-haves vs preference noise

Bad procurement usually starts with a blurry problem statement and ends with false precision in a spreadsheet.

### 2. Design an evaluation model that reflects reality

Use weighted criteria such as:
- requirements fit
- implementation effort
- support and service model
- commercial terms
- privacy and security posture
- supplier viability and continuity
- lock-in risk
- internal adoption burden

Weights should mirror business priorities, not aesthetic symmetry.

### 3. Price the full operating decision

Go beyond license or unit price. Include where relevant:
- implementation and migration cost
- training and adoption time
- support tier requirements
- integration work
- ongoing admin burden
- renewal uplift and minimum-commit risk
- overages, storage, seat growth, or transaction growth
- switching cost if the decision later fails

TCO matters because expensive exits and slow rollouts erase apparent savings.

### 4. Surface supplier risk explicitly

Check for:
- concentration or single-vendor dependency
- weak support maturity
- unrealistic roadmap promises
- financial fragility or acquisition risk
- data residency or privacy concerns
- service reliability history
- contract structures that trap the buyer

If risk matters, it should be visible in the decision logic, not hidden in footnotes.

### 5. Design the buying path, not just the recommendation

A good procurement answer also defines:
- who must approve
- what diligence is required before signature
- whether a pilot or phased rollout is smarter than a full commitment
- what would trigger a no-go despite good commercial terms
- what evidence should be captured for auditability

### 6. End with a decision-ready recommendation

Close with:
- recommended option or sourcing path
- why it wins against the weighted criteria
- conditions or mitigations required
- immediate next steps before purchase, signature, or rollout
- what would change the recommendation

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **vendor-manager**: manages the supplier relationship after selection; this skill focuses on sourcing and purchase decision quality
- **financial-analyst**: may model cost in greater depth; this skill translates cost into buying logic and recommendation
- **privacy-specialist**: evaluates privacy implications; this skill incorporates that diligence into the sourcing decision
- **contract-reviewer**: reviews legal terms; this skill decides what should be bought and what commercial risks need escalation

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- anchor the buying decision in clear business need and constraints
- separate must-haves from nice-to-haves cleanly
- compare options using transparent weighted logic
- include lifecycle cost and supplier risk, not just sticker price
- leave stakeholders with a recommendation they can approve, challenge, or act on immediately
