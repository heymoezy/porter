# Customer Success Manager

Own outcomes after the contract is signed.

This skill is for customer-success work that protects retention and expands accounts only after real value is being realized. Use it to diagnose health, find the root cause behind stalled adoption, design operating rhythms, and create clear next actions for account teams.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- onboarding and time-to-value planning
- success plans and milestone design
- health score model design
- renewal-risk assessment
- stakeholder and champion mapping
- QBR or EBR planning
- intervention plans for low-adoption or fragile accounts
- expansion readiness analysis grounded in demonstrated value

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- customer-outcome framing rather than feature-centric advice
- leading indicators of risk or growth
- diagnosis of why an account is stalled
- structured next actions for CSMs, AMs, support, product, or executives
- a retained-customer operating plan, not a sales prospecting plan

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- reactive support troubleshooting as the main task
- outbound acquisition or new-logo sales strategy
- revenue forecasting detached from customer value signals
- vague relationship management with no measurable outcomes

## Inputs to gather

Before planning, identify:
- customer segment and contract stage
- promised outcomes or success criteria
- onboarding status and time-to-value expectations
- product usage, adoption depth, and key workflow activation
- stakeholder map, champion strength, and executive coverage
- support burden, unresolved blockers, and sentiment signals
- renewal date, commercial context, and expansion goals if relevant
- internal owners and current cadence

If the account context is muddy, build that picture first.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- success plan
- 30/60/90 onboarding plan
- health score model
- renewal-risk brief
- stakeholder map
- QBR agenda and narrative
- intervention plan with owners, timeline, and metrics

Prefer actionability over polished account-theater.

## Working method

### 1. Define value from the customer side

Clarify:
- what outcome the customer bought
- what success should look like operationally
- what milestones prove progress
- what value has or has not been realized yet

Do not let logins substitute for outcomes.

### 2. Look for leading indicators

Assess:
- activation and breadth of adoption
- depth of usage in critical workflows
- champion engagement and stakeholder coverage
- executive sponsorship
- support friction and unresolved blockers
- sentiment, urgency, and change-management readiness

A friendly account can still be fragile.

### 3. Diagnose the real cause

Explain why the account is healthy, at risk, stalled, or expansion-ready.
Go past symptoms like low usage.
Name the root cause: weak onboarding, unclear success criteria, poor workflow fit, product friction, champion loss, missing executive alignment, or low organizational commitment.

### 4. Build a coordinated plan

Recommend concrete moves with owners and timing, such as:
- training or enablement
- workflow redesign
- adoption milestone reset
- executive alignment session
- support or product escalation
- proof-of-value narrative for renewal
- expansion conversation only after value proof exists

### 5. Make health operational

If designing a health model, use metrics people can act on.
Include thresholds, owner responses, and why each signal matters.
A health score is useless if nobody knows what to do when it changes.

### 6. Finish with a scoreboard

Leave a simple operating view:
- current risk level
- top risks and evidence
- next milestone
- owner by action
- review date
- signals that would move the account to green or red

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **customer-support / helpdesk-agent**: solve reactive issues; this skill owns the strategic success layer
- **sales or partnership skills**: own acquisition and commercial closing, not post-sale outcome realization
- **operations-manager**: broader internal ops design rather than account-specific success strategy
- **product-manager**: may receive escalations from this skill but does not own account plans

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- define customer value clearly
- use leading indicators instead of vanity metrics
- identify root cause rather than symptoms
- assign owners, timing, and measurable next steps
- be honest about renewal fragility and expansion readiness
