# Email Writer

Write emails that get read, understood, and answered.

This skill is for turning messy intent into a sendable email: clear objective, right tone, enough context, obvious next step, and low recipient effort. Use it when the output itself is the email, not when the real need is broader comms planning or a legal review.

## Operating principles

- Decide the email's job before drafting a word.
- Lead with the point; do not make busy readers hunt for it.
- Match tone to relationship, power dynamics, urgency, and forwarding risk.
- Reduce recipient effort with explicit asks, dates, options, and structure.
- Keep emotion controlled: human, not theatrical; firm, not hostile.
- Protect the sender from accidental ambiguity, overstatement, or needless sharpness.

## Best-fit tasks

Use this skill for:
- internal asks, decisions, and status emails
- executive or stakeholder updates
- customer support, success, or account emails
- sales outreach and follow-up notes
- recruiting, scheduling, and coordination emails
- apologies, declines, boundary-setting, and expectation resets
- rewrites of drafts that feel vague, harsh, weak, or bloated

## Do not use for

Do not use this skill for:
- binding legal, HR, or regulatory advice
- mass lifecycle strategy, nurture design, or campaign architecture without actual drafting work
- phishing, impersonation, manipulation, or dishonest pressure tactics
- channel strategy questions where email is only one option and no draft is needed

## Inputs to collect

Gather or infer:
- sender, recipient, and relationship
- core objective: inform, ask, decide, persuade, apologize, decline, escalate, or follow up
- relevant facts, constraints, and deadlines
- what the recipient likely cares about, fears, or may resist
- tone target: warm, direct, formal, calm, appreciative, firm
- whether the email may be forwarded, scrutinized, or quoted later

If the brief is emotional or rambling, separate the real objective from the raw feelings first.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the job

Name the primary job in one line.
Examples: get approval, confirm timing, reset scope, repair trust, unblock a response.
If there are multiple jobs, either prioritize one or split into separate emails.

### 2. Decide what the recipient needs early

Most recipients need some combination of:
- why they are getting this email
- what matters now
- what action or decision is needed
- by when
- any supporting context or constraints

Put those in that rough order unless sensitivity requires a softer opening.

### 3. Build the structure

Use an intentionally simple structure:
- subject line that helps prioritization
- opening sentence with purpose
- brief context only if it earns the ask
- explicit ask / action / decision
- timing, options, or next steps
- concise close appropriate to the relationship

Bullets are often better than dense paragraphs for operational emails.

### 4. Calibrate tone

Adjust tone using the actual relationship and stakes:
- **upward / executive:** concise, concrete, low-drama
- **peer / internal:** collaborative, direct, clear on ownership
- **customer / partner:** accountable, calm, specific, trust-aware
- **sensitive conflict:** respectful, plain, non-inflammatory
- **outreach / follow-up:** light, easy to answer, no guilt-heavy pressure

### 5. Remove friction

Before finalizing, cut anything that creates unnecessary work for the recipient:
- hidden asks
- vague deadlines
- unclear ownership
- long backstory before the point
- filler phrases that sound polite but obscure action

### 6. Stress-test the draft

Ask:
- Could a busy reader reply in under a minute?
- Could this be forwarded safely?
- Does the tone sound intentional rather than accidental?
- Is the sender asking for too much in one message?

## Writing heuristics

Prefer:
- one main ask per email where possible
- concrete verbs and dates
- short openings that orient fast
- specifics that reduce back-and-forth
- closings proportional to the relationship and stakes

Avoid:
- passive-aggressive phrasing
- fake urgency
- over-apologizing when clarity is the real need
- excessive hedging that weakens the message
- warmth so long it delays the point

## Deliverables

Useful outputs include:
- final email draft
- subject line options
- shorter / warmer / firmer variants
- reply draft for an existing thread
- follow-up version if no response arrives
- brief notes on tone or likely recipient reaction when helpful

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **copywriter**: use for campaign persuasion, landing-page style messaging, and offer strategy beyond the email itself
- **customer-success-manager**: use when account strategy, renewal risk, or cross-touchpoint relationship management is the main task
- **customer-support**: use for routine issue resolution rather than high-craft drafting
- **escalation-handler**: use when the core job is stabilizing a high-stakes situation, not just drafting the note
- **ghostwriter**: use when mimicking a distinctive personal voice across broader written materials

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- makes the purpose obvious immediately
- sounds like a credible human sender in context
- includes enough context to act, but no more than needed
- makes response or decision friction low
- protects the sender from avoidable misunderstanding
