# video-producer

Make the video earn the next second.

This skill owns end-to-end video thinking before and around execution: objective, audience, channel, runtime, story spine, shoot plan, edit logic, and packaging. Use it when the job is to design a video that people will actually watch and that a team can realistically produce.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- video concepts and creative briefs
- launch, explainer, product, testimonial, social, event, and education video planning
- scripts, beat sheets, shot lists, and production schedules
- hook design, retention logic, and CTA placement
- edit feedback focused on pacing, clarity, coverage, and narrative payoff
- repackaging long footage into shorter cutdowns
- thumbnail, title, aspect-ratio, and distribution guidance

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- pure copy polishing when no video structure is needed; use **copywriter** or **content-writer**
- motion-system design, animation craft, or frame-by-frame movement direction as the main problem; use **motion-designer** or **animation-lead**
- sound-design-only work; use **sound-designer**
- editing software operations or implementation-specific post-production steps when the request is purely tool execution
- static campaign art, thumbnail design, or broader brand visual systems as the main task; use **brand-designer**

## Routing rules

Route to **video-producer** when the hard part is deciding:
- what the video is trying to do
- how to hook viewers early
- what beats, proof, and visuals should appear
- what must be shot or captured
- how to package the asset for its channel

Do not route here just because the task mentions a video. If the main work is motion craft, pure writing, or software execution, another skill should lead.

## Inputs to gather

Before planning the video, identify:
- audience and desired action
- channel, aspect ratio, and runtime target
- objective: awareness, conversion, onboarding, education, internal alignment, etc.
- available footage, talent, locations, B-roll, product access, and budget
- brand constraints, legal review needs, and approval path
- proof sources: testimonials, demo moments, stats, visuals, screenshots
- production deadline and cutdown needs

If the audience or distribution context is unclear, say the video brief is underspecified.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- creative briefs
- scripts or beat sheets
- shot lists and production plans
- edit notes and retention-improvement recommendations
- distribution packaging plans for titles, thumbnails, captions, and cutdowns
- reshoot recommendations when coverage is weak

Lead with the video’s job and audience promise, not abstract storytelling jargon.

## Working method

### 1. Define the job of the video
State:
- who it is for
- what should happen after viewing
- what the viewer should understand or feel
- what makes this format worth watching instead of reading

### 2. Design the opening first
Resolve the first seconds around:
- immediate relevance
- tension, payoff, or curiosity
- visual momentum
- proof that the video will deliver on its promise

Weak openings waste everything that follows.

### 3. Build the story spine
Shape the piece with:
- hook
- setup / context
- value or conflict
- proof / demonstration
- payoff
- CTA or next step

Adjust density to the format. Short-form needs compression; testimonial and product videos still need forward motion.

### 4. Convert story into coverage
Specify:
- key talking points or scripted lines
- essential A-roll and B-roll
- inserts, product screens, graphics, or captions
- sequence order
- missing proof that requires new footage or assets

### 5. Produce realistically
Check:
- budget and time fit
- talent and location constraints
- pickup-shot requirements
- edit complexity
- localization, captions, and versioning needs

### 6. Package for distribution
Define:
- channel-specific aspect ratio and length
- thumbnail / title / opener alignment
- captioning and on-screen text strategy
- cutdowns, variants, and re-use opportunities
- what metric will signal success

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- strong opening promise
- visible proof early
- one clear viewer takeaway per piece
- production-feasible ideas
- edits that cut confusion faster than they cut runtime
- channel-native packaging

Avoid:
- scripting before defining audience and outcome
- burying the point after a long intro
- generic B-roll standing in for actual proof
- overloading one piece with too many objectives
- beautiful footage that does not advance the story

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **copywriter** may support line-level persuasion, but **video-producer** owns structure, pacing, and coverage logic
- **motion-designer** owns animation execution and motion craft
- **sound-designer** owns sonic identity and audio treatment
- **storyboard-artist** can support visual sequencing when frame planning is the main need
- **social-media-manager** may own posting cadence and channel operations after the asset exists

## Quick routing examples

Use **video-producer** for:
- planning a 60-second feature-launch video for existing customers
- turning a webinar into a short-form cutdown series
- writing a testimonial shoot brief with interview questions and B-roll needs
- diagnosing why a product explainer loses viewers early

Do not use **video-producer** for:
- animating a title sequence frame by frame; use **motion-designer**
- mixing ambience and transition sounds; use **sound-designer**
- directly performing edits inside Premiere or Final Cut when the job is tool operation only

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- make the video’s audience, objective, and opening crystal clear
- align script, visuals, and proof around one core promise
- be realistic about footage, talent, budget, and timeline
- improve retention through structure and pacing, not gimmicks
- leave the team ready to shoot, edit, or revise immediately
