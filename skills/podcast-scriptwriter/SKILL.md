# Podcast Scriptwriter

Write for the ear. Strong outputs guide the host through a clear beginning, middle, and end without sounding like a memo being read into a microphone. Optimize for spoken clarity, momentum, authenticity, and easy recording.

## Use this skill to
- write full podcast scripts for solo, narrative, or tightly timed shows
- create beat sheets, rundowns, and segment architecture
- shape interview outlines and question progression
- write intros, outros, transitions, sponsor reads, and CTAs
- tighten existing scripts so they sound more human when spoken
- adapt notes, research, or articles into audio-first structure

## Do not use this skill for
- designing the broader podcast production system or release workflow
- blog posts, essays, or web copy that will mainly be read rather than heard
- technical audio-editing instruction
- screenplay, documentary treatment, or voiceover work where podcast performance is not the target format

## Inputs to gather
Before writing, identify:
- audience and what they should feel, learn, or do by the end
- show format and host voice
- desired script tightness: full script, partial script, rundown, or interview scaffold
- target runtime and required segments
- guest context, story beats, research notes, or source material
- sponsor, CTA, disclaimer, or recurring ritual requirements

If the host voice is unknown, write clean spoken scaffolding and mark places for improvisation.

## Output shapes
Return whichever form best fits the job:
- full episode script
- beat sheet with timing
- interview architecture with question arcs
- intro / outro package
- sponsor-read options
- spoken-flow revision notes

Use clear beat labels, section timings, and optional trims when useful.

## Working method

### 1. Define the listener payoff
Name the payoff first:
- a new insight
- a compelling story
- tactical action steps
- a sharp interview perspective
- an emotional turn

Then build the structure to deliver that payoff without drift.

### 2. Choose the right script density
Match control to the job:
- **full script** for narrative, precision, compliance-sensitive wording, or tightly timed delivery
- **hybrid script** for guided structure with pockets of ad-lib room
- **rundown / beat sheet** for conversational hosts
- **interview scaffold** for guest-led episodes

Over-scripting kills energy. Under-structuring creates ramble.

### 3. Write for speech, not silent reading
Use:
- shorter sentences
- clean transitions
- one idea at a time
- words a host would actually say
- emphasis, pause, and contrast points

If a line is hard to hear once, it is too dense.

### 4. Engineer momentum
Open fast. Maintain movement with:
- hooks
- segment promises
- resets after tangents
- bridges into ads or CTAs
- summaries that reward attention

Every beat should earn the next minute.

### 5. Make the script easy to record and cut
Include what production needs:
- time estimates
- beat or segment labels
- optional trims
- alternate lines when useful
- pronunciation or context notes only when necessary

Good scripts reduce retakes and make editing cleaner.

### 6. Preserve the host's real voice
Tune language to the host:
- analytical vs playful
- polished vs conversational
- high-energy vs calm
- skeptical vs warm

A perfect script in the wrong voice is a bad script.

## Diagnostic lenses
Review the work through these lenses:
- hook strength
- spoken clarity
- pacing and breathability
- authenticity of voice
- interview question sequencing
- sponsor / CTA integration
- ease of recording and editing

## Adjacent skill boundaries
- **podcast-producer** designs the show system, cadence, and workflow; this skill writes the episode's spoken structure.
- **copywriter** writes to persuade readers; this skill writes to guide listeners.
- **story-editor** handles broader narrative critique; this skill converts narrative intent into recordable audio structure.

## Quality bar
A strong result should:
- sound natural out loud
- keep listener orientation and momentum high
- fit the host, format, and runtime
- leave the right amount of room for improvisation
- be easy to record, trim, and produce
