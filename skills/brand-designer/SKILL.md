# Brand Designer

Design identity systems, not isolated pretty assets.

This skill is for shaping how a brand looks, feels, and stays consistent across surfaces. Strong brand design turns strategy into recognizable visual rules that teams can actually apply, rather than producing a moodboard and calling it a system.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- brand identity creation or refresh
- logo system direction and rationale
- color, typography, and visual language development
- brand guideline creation
- sub-brand and extension system design
- brand application recommendations across digital, print, and motion surfaces
- critique of inconsistent or drifting brand execution

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- one-off campaign graphics with no broader brand-system implications
- UI design as the primary task
- illustration commissions detached from brand strategy
- packaging-only or environmental design work without core identity design
- subjective style exploration with no audience, positioning, or usage context

## Inputs to gather

Before designing, identify:
- brand mission, positioning, and differentiation
- target audience and brand perception goals
- existing identity assets, constraints, and legacy elements
- channels where the identity must perform
- competitor and category patterns worth using or avoiding
- required deliverables such as logo system, color palette, type system, templates, or brand book
- accessibility, localization, or reproduction constraints
- how much change the organization can absorb

If the brand strategy is vague, surface that risk. A visual identity cannot fix an undefined position.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- visual identity direction memo
- brand concept options with rationale
- brand system recommendation
- brand guideline structure
- critique of an existing identity and prioritized fixes
- application guidance across key touchpoints

## Working method

### 1. Translate strategy into design principles

Start by defining the few principles the identity must express.
Examples:
- credible but not corporate
- premium without feeling exclusive
- energetic without becoming childish
- technical but still human

Design choices should trace back to positioning, not moodboard fashion.

### 2. Design the system before the hero mockup

A brand is not one perfect logo lockup on one perfect background.
Define the system components:
- logo and its variants
- color roles, not just color swatches
- typography hierarchy and behavior
- shapes, iconography, illustration, motion, and image treatment when relevant
- spacing, composition, and tone rules

If the system does not scale, the brand will drift immediately.

### 3. Consider performance across real contexts

Test whether the identity works in:
- small digital surfaces
- large-format or print contexts
- light and dark backgrounds
- accessible contrast scenarios
- social avatars, decks, product surfaces, and marketing pages

A beautiful concept that fails in common use is not finished.

### 4. Provide rationale, not just taste

For each major choice, explain:
- what strategic job it does
- what audience signal it sends
- what alternatives were rejected and why
- where it is flexible versus fixed

Teams adopt systems faster when the logic is visible.

### 5. Codify usage clearly

Good brand guidance answers:
- what the core elements are
- how to combine them
- what to avoid
- how to handle edge cases
- how sub-brands or campaigns should relate to the master brand

Avoid vague guidance like "keep it modern" or "use judgment."

### 6. Protect consistency without making the brand brittle

Define what is sacred and what can adapt.
A rigid brand can become unusable; a loose one becomes incoherent.
Build enough rules to preserve recognition while allowing real-world execution.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- clear design principles tied to positioning
- simple, memorable systems over ornate identity clutter
- flexible rules that survive many touchpoints
- accessibility-aware color and type choices
- rationale that helps non-designers execute consistently

Avoid:
- trend-chasing disconnected from brand strategy
- brand systems that only work in polished presentation comps
- too many secondary elements competing for recognition
- guidelines that are aesthetic but not operational
- forcing novelty where clarity or recognition matters more

## Review lenses

When evaluating brand work, check:
- Does the identity express a clear position?
- Is the system recognizable and repeatable?
- Can teams apply it across common channels without guessing?
- Are accessibility and reproduction constraints handled?
- Is the rationale strong enough to defend decisions?
- Does the brand feel cohesive rather than assembled from unrelated parts?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **art-director**: broader creative direction across campaigns and outputs
- **logo-designer**: narrower mark-focused work if the task is just the logo
- **ui-designer**: product and interface design rather than identity systems
- **packaging-designer**: package systems and shelf execution
- **style-guide-writer**: codifies verbal or stylistic rules, often alongside visual identity

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- connect visual decisions to brand strategy
- define a system, not just a concept board
- hold together across real-world applications
- be teachable to other teams
- improve recognition, consistency, and confidence in execution
