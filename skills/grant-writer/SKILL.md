# Grant Writer

Use this skill for disciplined grant development.

Strong grant writing is not emotional pleading. It is proof of fit: this applicant, for this population, using this model, can deliver these outcomes within these rules and this budget.

## Gather the minimum context first

Identify:
- funder, program, and application stage
- eligibility rules, scoring criteria, and word or page limits
- applicant mission, track record, staffing, and delivery capacity
- target population, geography, and evidence of need
- proposed activities, timeline, partners, and implementation model
- outputs, outcomes, evaluation approach, and reporting expectations
- budget limits, allowable costs, match requirements, and required attachments
- what the user needs now: fit decision, outline, draft, revision, or compliance map

If key facts are missing, mark them as validation gaps instead of inventing them.

## Core workflow

1. **Check fit before drafting hard**
   - Confirm eligibility, mission alignment, population fit, and execution credibility.
   - Say no early if the opportunity is structurally weak.
2. **Map the application exactly**
   - Break the notice or prompt set into questions, scoring hooks, attachments, limits, and mandatory evidence.
   - Create a response plan before writing full prose.
3. **Build the logic chain**
   - Define the problem, why it matters now, why this applicant is credible, what will be done, for whom, and what will change.
   - Keep the theory of change readable, not academic theater.
4. **Separate activities, outputs, and outcomes**
   - Activities = what the program does.
   - Outputs = what gets delivered or how many are served.
   - Outcomes = what changes for participants, systems, or communities.
5. **Keep every claim supportable**
   - Use real evidence, real capacity, real partnerships, and realistic numbers.
   - Flag missing proof points and budget assumptions for confirmation.

## What good output looks like

Return practical deliverables such as:
- pursue / defer recommendation
- requirement matrix aligned to prompts and criteria
- proposal outline by section
- draft narrative answers
- need statement with evidence gaps called out
- goals, outputs, outcomes, and evaluation language
- budget narrative support notes and validation flags

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- fit discipline before prose polish
- direct alignment to funder language and priorities
- credible specificity over inspirational vagueness
- reviewer-friendly structure and plain language
- measurable outcomes linked to implementation reality

Avoid:
- generic mission filler copied into every answer
- invented impact numbers, partners, testimonials, or capacity claims
- blurring activities with outcomes
- budgets that imply impossible delivery models
- ignoring attachments, limits, or scoring criteria

## Boundary calls

Use adjacent skills instead when needed:
- **government-specialist** when the main challenge is public-sector procedural interpretation
- **proposal-writer** or **proposal-manager** for broader multi-author submission orchestration
- **nonprofit-advisor** for organizational fundraising strategy beyond one application
- **financial-analyst** or **budget-focused** skills when cost modeling is the main work

## Final check

Before finishing, verify:
- the opportunity is actually a fit
- every answer maps to a prompt or score-driving criterion
- outcomes are measurable and believable
- unsupported facts are flagged, not smuggled in
- the narrative sounds credible for the actual applicant and budget
