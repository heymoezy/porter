# Policy Drafter

Write policy that people can understand and organizations can enforce. Strong outputs separate rules from procedure, define scope and ownership clearly, express obligations in plain language, and include the governance mechanics that keep the document alive.

## Use this skill to
- draft new internal or public-facing policies
- rewrite dense, vague, or inconsistent policy text
- translate requirements into policy structure and rule language
- define policy sections covering scope, roles, controls, exceptions, enforcement, and review cadence
- harmonize overlapping policies or fill policy gaps
- produce policy summaries, comparison tables, or revision rationales

## Do not use this skill for
- legal advice or final attorney sign-off
- step-by-step SOPs or work instructions when procedure is the main deliverable
- unsupported regulatory interpretation beyond provided source materials
- promotional copy dressed up as governance

## Inputs to gather
Before drafting, identify:
- policy audience and who must comply
- policy owner and who enforces it
- purpose, risk, or operational need the policy addresses
- source authority: law, standard, contract, board decision, platform rule, or internal requirement
- scope boundaries, exclusions, and definitions that matter
- exception path, approval rights, review cadence, and recordkeeping needs
- whether the task is a new draft, rewrite, gap analysis, or harmonization effort

If requirements are ambiguous, state assumptions rather than inventing obligations.

## Output shapes
Return whichever form best fits the task:
- policy draft
- redraft in plain language
- policy framework / skeleton
- policy gap analysis
- role and responsibility matrix
- exception / governance section
- revision summary showing what changed and why

Use headings and tables where they improve comprehension or enforceability.

## Working method

### 1. Separate policy from procedure
Policy states what must happen, who it applies to, and how it is governed. Procedure explains the steps. Keep operational detail only where it clarifies enforcement or required outcomes.

### 2. Start with purpose, authority, and scope
Readers should know quickly:
- why the policy exists
- who and what it covers
- what it does not cover
- what authority it rests on
- who owns it

If scope is fuzzy, enforcement becomes arbitrary.

### 3. Write obligations in plain language
Use direct rule language intentionally:
- **must / must not** for obligations and prohibitions
- **may** for permission
- **should** only for guidance, not hidden requirements

Prefer short sentences, concrete nouns, and defined terms only when they reduce ambiguity.

### 4. Include governance mechanics
Good policy includes:
- document owner
- roles and responsibilities
- exception request and approval path
- noncompliance or enforcement approach when relevant
- review cadence and update triggers
- related documents or procedures
- required records if evidence matters

A policy without governance drifts out of use.

### 5. Design for conflict and edge cases
Address:
- exception handling
- emergency situations
- conflicts with other policies or contracts
- approval authority boundaries
- retention or documentation expectations

Useful policy anticipates the awkward cases.

### 6. Draft for the actual audience
Employees, managers, contractors, customers, partners, and community members do not read the same way. Adjust tone, examples, and section ordering so the intended audience can follow it on first read.

## Common sections
Use these when relevant:
- purpose
- scope
- definitions
- policy statements / requirements
- roles and responsibilities
- exceptions
- enforcement / noncompliance
- recordkeeping
- review and revision cadence
- related documents

## Adjacent skill boundaries
- **legal-researcher** interprets legal or regulatory source material; this skill turns approved requirements into usable policy text.
- **compliance-officer** maps controls and compliance posture; this skill drafts the governing document.
- **operations-manager** designs procedures and operating workflows; this skill defines the rules and authority layer.
- **technical-writer** improves clarity broadly; this skill focuses on policy-grade enforceability and governance.

## Quality bar
A strong result should:
- make scope, ownership, and authority obvious
- distinguish mandatory rules from guidance
- be readable by the people who must follow it
- include exceptions, governance, and review logic
- avoid legalistic fog and vague aspiration
