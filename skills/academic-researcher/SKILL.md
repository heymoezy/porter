# Academic Researcher

Produce disciplined, verifiable research outputs. The goal is not to sound scholarly; it is to build a traceable evidence base, synthesize it honestly, and communicate what is known, contested, and still uncertain.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- literature reviews
- systematic or semi-systematic evidence scans
- scoping reviews
- annotated bibliographies
- source credibility assessment
- synthesis memos comparing findings across studies
- research question framing and methodology planning
- evidence-backed background sections for reports, papers, proposals, or product decisions

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- academic or institutional sources rather than generic web content
- transparent source selection criteria
- explicit methodology and limitations
- citation-ready summaries
- comparison of multiple papers, studies, or evidence sources
- distinction between strong evidence, weak evidence, and open questions

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- lightweight fact lookup with no need for scholarly rigor
- PR, copywriting, or persuasive messaging that is not evidence-led
- purely commercial market research unless the task specifically needs academic literature
- unsupported speculation dressed up as “research”

## Inputs to gather

Before synthesizing, identify:
- the research question or decision question
- scope boundaries: topic, population, geography, time window, domain
- evidence type needed: peer-reviewed, systematic review, policy paper, working paper, standards body, grey literature
- expected output: memo, annotated bibliography, evidence table, narrative review, gap analysis
- required citation style if relevant
- time/quality bar: quick evidence scan vs deep review

If the user has not specified scope, propose one before proceeding.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- research plan or protocol
- source shortlist with inclusion rationale
- evidence table
- annotated bibliography
- synthesis memo with thematic findings
- limitations and confidence assessment
- open questions / future research directions

Always make it easy to verify where claims came from.

## Working method

### 1. Frame the question precisely

Turn vague prompts into a researchable question. Define:
- topic
- target population or context
- key concepts and synonyms
- time horizon
- what counts as a relevant source

Where useful, structure the question using a simple framework such as:
- concept / population / intervention / outcome
- or topic / scope / comparison / decision to inform

### 2. Choose the review type

Pick the appropriate rigor level:
- **Quick evidence scan**: fast orientation, limited source set, explicit that coverage is incomplete
- **Narrative review**: thematic synthesis across sources without claiming systematic completeness
- **Scoping review**: map the landscape, themes, methods, and gaps
- **Systematic-style review**: explicit search logic, inclusion/exclusion criteria, study screening, transparent synthesis method

Do not imply a systematic review unless the process actually supports that claim.

### 3. Define inclusion and exclusion rules

State the rules before synthesis when possible:
- publication type
- date range
- language
- geography or sector
- study design or evidence level
- relevance thresholds
- exclusion reasons (for example opinion-only, duplicate, outdated, off-topic, weak methods)

Transparent criteria matter more than false completeness.

### 4. Search broadly, then narrow intelligently

Use multiple source classes where needed:
- peer-reviewed journals
- review papers and meta-analyses
- academic books/chapters when relevant
- preprints with caution and explicit labeling
- government, standards, NGO, or institutional reports
- conference proceedings for fast-moving fields

Prefer the highest-quality available evidence, but note when only weaker evidence exists.

### 5. Evaluate source quality, not just relevance

Assess sources on:
- authority and venue credibility
- methodological clarity
- sample size / design appropriateness where relevant
- recency
- reproducibility or transparency
- bias, conflicts, or agenda
- whether the conclusion matches the data

Do not mechanically trust “peer-reviewed” as a magic seal. Explain quality concerns.

### 6. Extract evidence in a structured way

Capture details consistently, such as:
- citation
- source type
- question addressed
- method/design
- setting/sample
- key findings
- limitations
- strength/confidence
- relevance to the user’s question

Use tables whenever comparing more than a few sources.

### 7. Synthesize, don’t just stack summaries

A good academic research output should:
- identify patterns across sources
- surface disagreement and why it exists
- separate descriptive findings from causal claims
- highlight gaps, not just consensus
- note where evidence is thin, outdated, or context-bound

Prefer statements like:
- “Most sources agree…”
- “Evidence is mixed because…”
- “This conclusion appears limited to…”
- “The strongest available evidence suggests…”

### 8. Be explicit about confidence and limitations

Always include:
- scope limitations
- missing evidence
- possible bias in the available literature
- confidence level by claim or theme
- whether findings are transferable to the target context

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **research-analyst**: broader and less scholarly; may use mixed web/business sources. This skill is for academic rigor and evidence discipline.
- **market-researcher**: customer/market/competitive research. This skill is literature- and evidence-synthesis-first.
- **policy-researcher / legal-researcher / clinical-researcher**: specialized domain methods may apply. Use them when the domain itself drives the methodology.
- **technical-writer**: may package findings into polished deliverables, but this skill builds the evidence base.

## Citation and evidence rules

- Cite enough detail to verify the source.
- Distinguish direct findings from your interpretation.
- Label preprints, opinion pieces, and grey literature clearly.
- Do not invent studies, journals, volumes, issue numbers, or DOIs.
- If access is incomplete, say so.

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- state the question and scope clearly
- use explicit source-selection logic
- compare sources rather than list them
- identify both consensus and disagreement
- make limitations impossible to miss
- remain useful to a real decision-maker, not just academically tidy
