# Editorial Planner

Build the publishing system, not a pile of topic ideas.

This skill turns business goals, audience needs, and production constraints into an editorial operating plan: what to publish, why it matters, how pieces connect, when they should ship, and what to stop making.

## Use this skill to

- build monthly, quarterly, or campaign-based editorial calendars
- define content pillars, themes, and recurring series
- prioritize story backlogs and kill low-value ideas
- sequence blog, newsletter, social, webinar, case-study, and launch content
- align editorial output with demand gen, education, retention, trust, or category goals
- create editorial operating rules that downstream writers and editors can execute

## Do not use this skill for

- drafting the final article, email, script, or post with no planning component
- pure technical SEO auditing without editorial strategy choices
- paid-media planning or ad trafficking
- newsroom reporting or PR/media-relations workflows

## Gather first

Clarify:
- business objective: awareness, pipeline, launch support, education, retention, trust, category leadership
- target audience segments, moments of need, objections, and information gaps
- current content inventory, performance patterns, and missing proof assets
- channels, formats, approval paths, and team capacity
- key dates: launches, events, seasonality, campaigns, regulations, dependencies
- distinctive point of view: what the team can say better, sooner, or more credibly than others

If capacity is tight, narrow the plan. Fewer pieces with clear editorial intent beat a crowded calendar nobody can ship.

## Deliverables

Produce one or more of:
- editorial thesis and planning memo
- monthly or quarterly calendar
- pillar / theme architecture
- prioritized story backlog
- launch or campaign publishing sequence
- recurring-series design
- editorial governance notes and commissioning cues

## Working method

### 1. Start at the intersection

The plan should sit where audience demand meets business need.
If it only serves the business, it reads like promotion.
If it only serves the audience, it may never move outcomes.

### 2. Define pillars with real edges

Pillars should exclude as much as they include.
Give each one a job: teach, persuade, prove, announce, retain, or reframe.
Avoid giant buckets that make every idea look acceptable.

### 3. Plan arcs, not isolated posts

Strong editorial systems compound.
Sequence pieces so they build context and momentum:
primer → sharp angle → proof asset → objection handling → recap or update.

### 4. Match ambition to throughput

A calendar is only good if the team can actually ship it.
Account for research load, approvals, SME access, design dependency, and channel adaptation effort.

### 5. Balance the portfolio

Most strong programs mix:
- evergreen utility
- timely commentary
- strategic opinion
- proof assets such as case studies, customer stories, or data
- campaign / launch support

Do not let one category dominate by accident.

### 6. Encode distinctiveness

A useful plan tells downstream creators what angle to pursue, what clichés to avoid, what evidence to include, and why the piece deserves attention now.
Topic names alone are weak planning.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- strong angles over generic nouns
- repeatable formats that lower planning drag
- backlog triage, not backlog hoarding
- sequencing logic across channels
- explicit deprioritization of weak ideas

Avoid:
- calendars with no editorial thesis
- publishing volume for its own sake
- channeling every piece everywhere by default
- confusing keyword buckets with editorial strategy
- ignoring production reality and approvals

## Output shape

Use this flow when it fits:
1. Goals, audiences, and constraints
2. Editorial thesis
3. Recommended pillars, themes, or series
4. Calendar / sequencing plan
5. Capacity notes, tradeoffs, and what to cut
6. Immediate briefs or next actions

## Adjacent boundaries

- **content-writer**: drafts the actual piece
- **copywriter**: writes conversion-oriented messaging assets
- **seo-specialist**: deeper search-demand and optimization work
- **social-media-manager**: channel execution and community rhythm

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- creates focus instead of idea sprawl
- makes the rationale for each piece obvious
- reflects real throughput and constraints
- produces differentiated angles, not filler topics
- gives editors and writers something they can execute immediately
