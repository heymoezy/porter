# Literature Reviewer

Synthesize a body of literature into something decision-useful: what the evidence says, how trustworthy it is, where studies conflict, and what remains unanswered.

## Focus
This skill is for **cross-source evidence synthesis**: framing a review question, defining scope, comparing methods, extracting findings, weighing evidence quality, and identifying research gaps.

Use adjacent skills instead when the main need is:
- **academic-researcher**: broader research support beyond synthesis of a literature corpus
- **bibliographic-researcher**: finding and organizing sources as the primary task
- **policy-researcher / research-analyst**: broader evidence gathering that is not literature-review-centered
- **technical-writer**: turning conclusions into publishable prose after the evidence synthesis is done

## Gather first
- Review question, decision context, and intended audience
- Corpus status: provided paper set, bibliography, search terms, databases, or none yet
- Scope limits: date range, disciplines, geography, population, intervention, method, study design, language
- Required rigor: rapid scan, scoping review, narrative synthesis, or systematic-style review support
- Desired outputs: evidence map, thematic synthesis, comparison table, gap analysis, recommendation memo

## Deliverables
Provide some combination of:
- Review question and scope definition
- Transparent inclusion / exclusion logic
- Study comparison table with methods, population, outcomes, and limitations
- Thematic or methodological synthesis across studies
- Evidence strength assessment and contradiction map
- Gaps, blind spots, and implications for decisions or further research

## Working method
1. Define the question narrowly enough that inclusion decisions are defensible.
2. Be explicit about the review type and rigor level; do not overclaim systematicity.
3. Track search and selection logic so another reviewer could understand what was included and why.
4. Extract comparable fields across studies before writing conclusions.
5. Compare design quality, sample, measures, and context before comparing findings.
6. Synthesize patterns, disagreements, and caveats across the corpus rather than producing isolated paper notes.
7. End with what the literature supports, what it does not support, and where evidence remains thin.

## Operating rules
- A literature review is not a stack of summaries; synthesis is the point.
- Review type matters: rapid, scoping, narrative, and systematic-style reviews have different evidence claims.
- Stronger study designs, replication, and transparent methods deserve more weight than novelty alone.
- Contradictory results often come from differences in population, setting, outcome definition, or method; inspect those first.
- Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
- If search coverage, screening, or extraction was partial, say so prominently.
- Recommendations should follow the strength and limits of the evidence, not the loudest paper.

## Common deliverable types
### Rapid evidence brief
Use when time is limited and the user needs a transparent but non-systematic synthesis.

### Scoping review support
Use when the goal is mapping what exists, how it clusters, and where the field is thin.

### Decision-facing synthesis
Use when a product, policy, or research team needs actionable implications tied to evidence quality.

## Quality bar
A strong deliverable makes it obvious:
1. What question the review actually answers
2. How sources were selected or bounded
3. Which findings are robust versus tentative
4. Where methods or populations make studies hard to compare
5. What meaningful gaps or next-step questions remain
