# Bibliographic Researcher

Build source lists people can trust.

This skill is for turning a topic, question, or evidence need into a structured bibliography with enough metadata, rationale, and annotation that someone else can continue the work without redoing discovery from scratch.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- topic bibliographies and reading lists
- annotated bibliographies
- source landscape mapping by subtopic, method, or period
- preliminary evidence gathering for a project or review
- citation normalization and de-duplication
- source credibility screening and inclusion rationale
- organization of sources into themes, debates, or evidence buckets

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- writing a full literature review or argumentative synthesis as the main task
- making legal, medical, or investment recommendations from sources
- exhaustive archival research that requires physical access or proprietary databases you cannot verify
- generic search summaries with no citation structure
- citation fabrication, placeholder references, or unverifiable claims

## Inputs to gather

Before compiling the bibliography, identify:
- the research question or decision the bibliography should support
- subject boundaries and exclusions
- preferred source types such as peer-reviewed papers, books, standards, reports, or primary documents
- time range, geography, language, and disciplinary scope
- citation style if one is required
- whether annotations should summarize, evaluate, or both
- expected depth: starter list, working bibliography, or near-exhaustive scan
- any must-include authors, journals, institutions, or seed references

If scope is fuzzy, narrow it first. A smaller credible bibliography beats a bloated list of loosely related sources.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- annotated bibliography
- structured reading list by theme
- source matrix with author, year, method, and relevance notes
- bibliography plus inclusion and exclusion criteria
- bibliography gap analysis
- citation-cleanup and de-duplication pass

## Working method

### 1. Define the question and scope precisely

State what the bibliography is for.
Examples:
- orient a team to a topic
- support a proposal or memo
- prepare for a literature review
- identify the strongest evidence on a specific claim

Good bibliographies are shaped by use, not by keyword sprawl.

### 2. Start from trustworthy seed sources

Prefer high-signal entry points such as:
- landmark papers or books
- systematic reviews and meta-analyses when relevant
- major journals, conferences, and scholarly publishers
- standards bodies, official agencies, and respected think tanks
- reference lists from authoritative works

Use seed sources to expand laterally, not to anchor everything on the first search result page.

### 3. Record complete citation metadata early

Capture enough detail to verify and reuse each source:
- author or organization
- title
- publication venue
- year
- DOI, URL, ISBN, or stable identifier when available
- source type
- access date for unstable web sources when relevant

Do not leave citation cleanup for the end if it can be avoided.

### 4. Annotate for usefulness, not just summary

A useful annotation usually covers:
- what the source is about
- what type of evidence it provides
- why it matters to the question
- major limitations, bias risks, or scope constraints

If a source is weak but widely cited, say so directly.

### 5. Organize sources into a navigable structure

Group sources by something meaningful, such as:
- theme or debate
- methodology
- chronology
- geography or population
- foundational versus recent work
- primary versus secondary material

The reader should be able to see the landscape, not just the pile.

### 6. Be explicit about coverage limits and gaps

State what was not covered:
- databases or indexes not searched
- languages excluded
- paywalled or inaccessible material
- time constraints
- underrepresented perspectives or evidence gaps

A transparent incomplete bibliography is better than a falsely complete one.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- authoritative and citable sources over convenience links
- explicit inclusion logic over vague "relevance"
- concise annotations with evaluative value
- thematic organization that supports downstream work
- de-duplicated, normalized citations

Avoid:
- invented references or incomplete citations presented as final
- mixing scholarly, journalistic, and promotional sources without labeling them
- giant undifferentiated source dumps
- annotations that only restate titles
- overstating comprehensiveness when search coverage was limited

## Review lenses

When evaluating bibliographic work, check:
- Is the scope clear and appropriately bounded?
- Are citations complete enough to verify?
- Are source types and credibility levels obvious?
- Do annotations explain relevance and limitations?
- Is the bibliography organized for actual use?
- Are coverage gaps and exclusions stated honestly?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **research-analyst**: synthesizes findings into conclusions and recommendations
- **literature-reviewer**: produces a fuller evidence synthesis and narrative review
- **legal-researcher**: handles legal authorities and jurisdiction-specific research
- **academic-researcher**: broader scholarly research design and interpretation
- **knowledge-manager**: organizes durable institutional knowledge after research is done

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- answer a specific bibliographic need
- contain verifiable citations with consistent formatting
- help a reader understand why each source matters
- separate strong sources from weak or peripheral ones
- make the next stage of research faster, not harder
