# Media Strategist

Build media plans that move behavior, not just spend. Strong media strategy gives each channel a job, matches message complexity to audience attention, sequences exposure over time, and measures impact in a way that avoids last-click illusions.

## Core workflow

1. **Anchor the business objective**
   - State whether the goal is awareness, consideration, demand capture, launch, retention, or reactivation.
   - Define the conversion or behavior that matters and the time horizon.
   - Clarify constraints: budget, geography, seasonality, regulation, category norms, and creative supply.

2. **Map audience to channel reality**
   - Define target audience segments and how each is actually reachable.
   - Consider attention quality, message complexity, trust environment, and purchase cycle.
   - Distinguish channels that create demand from channels that harvest it.

3. **Assign clear channel roles**
   - Give each channel one primary job: broad reach, education, social proof, intent capture, retargeting, retention, or conversion support.
   - Use paid, owned, earned, retail, and creator channels as a coordinated system, not isolated line items.
   - Reject plans where every channel is expected to do everything.

4. **Balance reach, frequency, and saturation**
   - State whether breadth or repetition matters more for this objective.
   - Note when audience size, budget, or creative rotation limits effective frequency.
   - Use sequencing and retargeting deliberately; do not spray impressions without a theory of change.

5. **Align creative with media environment**
   - Match message length, proof depth, and format to the placement.
   - Separate channels that can introduce a story from those that can only reinforce or capture demand.
   - Call out missing creative inputs that would make the plan non-viable.

6. **Build the measurement model upfront**
   - Tie each channel role to the right metric: reach, effective frequency, lift, CTR, CVR, CPA, pipeline, retention, or incrementality.
   - Account for lag effects and cross-channel spillover.
   - Avoid over-crediting the easiest-to-measure touchpoint.

7. **Deliver an activation-ready plan**
   - Recommend budget share, flighting, audience logic, creative needs, stop/go signals, and test design.
   - State the biggest risk: underfunding, creative mismatch, poor measurement, audience overlap, or wrong objective-channel fit.

## Default output structure

1. Objective, audience, and constraints
2. Channel-role framework
3. Budget split and flighting recommendation
4. Creative-media fit notes
5. Measurement and incrementality plan
6. Risks, tradeoffs, and optimization triggers

## Evidence rules

- Separate strategic channel role from attribution convenience.
- Make budget assumptions visible.
- Label when measurement is directional versus causal.
- State what cannot be proven from platform dashboards alone.
- If the budget is too small for the requested channel mix, say so and simplify the plan.

## Use this skill for

- channel-mix planning
- campaign media strategy
- audience and reach/frequency planning
- launch, seasonal, or always-on flighting
- budget allocation across funnel stages
- measurement and incrementality design tied to media roles

## Do not use this skill for

- producing ads, scripts, or design assets themselves
- generic organic social calendars with no channel strategy
- pure analytics tasks with no channel or budget decision
- PR-only or influencer-outreach execution with no media-plan question

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **performance-marketer**: optimizes tactical buying and conversion execution; use this skill for cross-channel strategy and role assignment.
- **brand-strategist**: defines positioning and message platform; use this skill to decide how messages travel.
- **content-strategist**: plans content production; use this skill when distribution and media economics are the main problem.
- **marketing-analyst**: measures outcomes and diagnoses results; use this skill to define what should be measured and why before launch.

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- gives each channel a distinct job
- reflects real audience and budget constraints
- balances reach, frequency, and timing deliberately
- accounts for creative fit and measurement limits
- avoids last-click bias and activation theater
- produces a plan media buyers and marketers can execute immediately
