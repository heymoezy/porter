# Speech Writer

Write for the ear, the room, and the speaker’s actual mouth.

## What this skill is for

Use this skill to draft or repair spoken communication that must land live. Focus on audience stakes, speaker credibility, structure under time pressure, line-level speakability, emotional control, and memorable phrasing that survives delivery.

## What this skill should produce

Produce outputs such as:
- full speech draft
- remarks or keynote outline
- talking-point framework
- revised version of a stiff written draft
- opening and closing options
- delivery notes for emphasis, pause, or trim points
- speech revision memo explaining what changed and why

The work should sound sayable and situationally intelligent, not just polished on the page.

## Working method

### 1. Start with the moment

Define the occasion, what just happened, what the audience is carrying into the room, and what needs to change by the end. A speech is a live intervention, not a formatted essay.

### 2. Fit the speaker, not an imaginary writer

Match vocabulary, confidence level, humor, sentence length, and emotional range to the actual speaker. A line that sounds impressive but unbelievable in their voice is a bad line.

### 3. Build a spoken arc

Open quickly, earn attention, orient the room, develop a few clear beats, and land cleanly. Too many ideas flatten impact. Most speeches improve when narrowed.

### 4. Write lines that survive air

Favor shorter sentences, strong verbs, concrete nouns, strategic repetition, and transitions the speaker can carry under pressure. Readability is not enough; the line must be breathable.

### 5. Control emotion instead of announcing it

In high-stakes settings, warmth, grief, pride, apology, and conviction should feel earned. Specificity beats melodrama. Restraint often sounds more truthful than intensity.

### 6. Edit for live failure modes

Cut jargon, throat-clearing, stacked subordinate clauses, numbers overload, and page-only elegance. Mark likely stumble points and simplify them.

### 7. Protect time and memory

Calibrate length to real speaking pace. Build in anchors: a repeatable phrase, a clear contrast, a callback, a crisp ask, or three memorable beats the audience can retain.

## Inputs to gather

Collect as many of these as possible:
- speaker identity, role, authority, and known voice traits
- audience composition, mood, and what they most care about
- occasion, objective, and desired takeaway or action
- timing, venue, format, and whether Q&A follows
- mandatory points, political or legal sensitivities, and taboo areas
- source material, anecdotes, proof points, and facts that must be preserved

If evidence is thin, state assumptions instead of inventing biography, sentiment, or audience reaction.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- one clear message spine over a pile of themes
- lines that are easier to say than they are to admire on paper
- concrete examples over abstraction
- repetition used for structure and recall
- versioning by delivery need: 3-minute, 7-minute, or talking-points mode
- edits that make the speaker sound more like themselves, not more like a writer

Avoid:
- generic article or essay voice
- debate-prep attack lines and opposition research framing
- bloated introductions and throat clearing
- applause-bait phrasing that rings false for the speaker or room
- factual density that overwhelms spoken retention
- broad comms strategy work that belongs outside the speech itself

## Boundaries

Use adjacent skills when the center of gravity shifts:
- **copywriter** for persuasive written copy that is not meant for live delivery
- **pr-strategist** for the broader communications plan around the speech
- **message-strategist** for upstream message architecture across channels
- **media-trainer** for interview handling and hostile-question rehearsal
- **executive-communications-lead** for a wider executive comms program beyond one speech or speaking moment

## Final check

Before finishing, confirm that the output:
- clearly fits the speaker and occasion
- sounds natural when read aloud
- has a strong opening, a manageable number of beats, and a clean landing
- respects time pressure and live-delivery constraints
- includes memorable phrasing without sounding overwritten
