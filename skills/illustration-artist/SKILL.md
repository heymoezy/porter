# Illustration Artist

Create illustrations that communicate a specific idea, feeling, and point of view—not just a generic “nice-looking” image.

## Start with the communication job
Clarify:
- what the illustration must make the audience understand or feel
- where it will appear: app, landing page, article, deck, ad, packaging, social, motion frame
- what brand traits it must reinforce
- what it must not look like
- whether the work is a one-off hero, a reusable system, or a narrative series

## Pick the style lane on purpose
Do not stop at adjectives like playful, premium, or modern.
Specify:
- line quality,
- shape language,
- palette logic,
- texture treatment,
- level of realism,
- character proportions,
- lighting mood,
- background density,
- composition energy.

The style should serve the message. If the message is complex, simplify the style enough to preserve readability.

## Build the composition around meaning
Use composition to control the story:
1. define focal point,
2. decide camera distance and angle,
3. stage hierarchy with scale, contrast, and negative space,
4. remove decorative elements that compete with the point,
5. check crop behavior across likely placements.

When relevant, think in series: recurring motifs, palette discipline, scene rhythm, and how one image relates to the next.

## Design narrative and symbolic clarity
Strong illustration direction names:
- the core scene,
- who or what is active,
- what tension exists,
- what symbolic devices carry the concept,
- what should remain literal versus metaphorical.

If the concept is abstract, give it a visual anchor so it does not dissolve into mood-board mush.

## Produce handoff-quality briefs
Useful outputs include:
- art direction brief,
- illustration concept options,
- prompt-ready visual directions,
- scene breakdowns,
- character and environment specs,
- critique with concrete fixes,
- system rules for an illustration family.

Describe what to draw and why it should be drawn that way.

## Adjacent skill boundaries
- Use **icon-designer** for small symbolic interface graphics.
- Use **art-director** when the task spans a broader campaign or multi-asset creative system beyond illustration.
- Use **storyboard-artist** when the core need is shot-by-shot sequencing for motion.

## Guardrails
- Do not confuse style with concept.
- Do not overload scenes with symbolic clutter.
- Do not reference famous artists so specifically that the output becomes derivative.
- If characters or cultural motifs are involved, call out representation and stereotype risks.
- If the illustration must animate or resize, design for those constraints early.

## Quality bar
The result should give a human illustrator, designer, or image model enough direction to produce work that feels intentional, distinctive, and on-brief.
