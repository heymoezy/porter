# Storyboard Artist

Make the sequence legible before production burns time and money.

## Use this skill to

- convert a script, treatment, or scene brief into a shot-by-shot board plan
- clarify geography, screen direction, eyelines, entrances, exits, and blocking
- map pacing, emphasis, reveals, and transitions across a sequence
- pressure-test coverage for ads, films, explainers, demos, social videos, or animation
- revise weak boards that feel confusing, repetitive, flat, or overcovered
- create animatic-friendly panel descriptions with action, framing, and timing intent

## Do not use this skill for

- final polished illustration or key art rendering
- writing the script from scratch when story structure is the real problem
- concept art, character design, or environment design without sequence planning
- deep lensing / lighting packages when the real need is cinematography execution

## Inputs to gather

Collect the minimum needed to board intelligently:

- script pages, scene synopsis, or beat-by-beat action
- format and runtime target: film, ad, explainer, animation, demo, social short
- objective of the scene: orient, reveal, persuade, escalate, explain, transition
- emotional beats and priority moments
- cast, props, setting, and practical production constraints
- style references, aspect ratio, and whether motion or still boards are needed

If key details are missing, state assumptions explicitly. Do not quietly invent props, geography, or impossible action.

## Deliverables

Return one or more of these:

- storyboard panel plan with panel numbers
- shot-by-shot sequence breakdown
- blocking and camera movement notes
- visual continuity / geography notes
- revision memo on what to cut, combine, or redraw
- animatic-ready board descriptions with duration feel and transitions

## Working method

### 1. Identify the audience question each beat must answer

Each panel should help the viewer understand one of these: where are we, who matters, what changed, what should we feel, what do we need to notice next.

### 2. Solve geography before style

Establish spatial logic early. Show entrances, exits, eyelines, axis, and relationship shifts before chasing dramatic angles. If geography is muddy, the sequence will stay muddy.

### 3. Choose coverage by narrative job

Use shot size and angle to do work:
- wide for orientation and staging
- medium for interaction and power dynamics
- close for decision, emotion, or reveal
- insert for critical detail only when the detail changes understanding

Do not add panels that merely repeat the previous one with no new information.

### 4. Design rhythm across the whole sequence

Board in sequences, not isolated frames. Control pace by varying scale, density, movement, and transition logic. Repetition is acceptable only when it builds tension or comedic pattern.

### 5. Board for shootability or animateability

Check whether the plan fits location limits, performer action, edit needs, animation labor, prop continuity, and likely setup count. Elegant impossible boards are bad boards.

### 6. Mark moments of emphasis and compression

Call out where the sequence should breathe, where it should cut fast, and where one panel can imply action instead of showing every step.

### 7. Critique for clarity, not beauty alone

Judge whether another human could produce from the board. A rough but readable board beats a gorgeous vague one.

## Heuristics

- Prefer visual cause-and-effect over decorative composition.
- Preserve screen direction unless a motivated break improves comprehension.
- Use camera movement sparingly; movement without purpose weakens attention.
- Combine panels when coverage is redundant.
- Add inserts only when the edit or story truly depends on them.
- Flag continuity hazards: hand-offs, prop state, eyelines, staging jumps, impossible reverses.
- For ads and explainers, ensure the product/demo object stays readable, not just cinematic.

## Failure modes to catch

- geography only becomes clear after external explanation
- too many similar mediums with no pacing contrast
- reveal panels happen before the setup earns them
- action coverage ignores edit bridges or transition needs
- boards imply impossible resets, VFX, or camera positions
- every beat is shown literally instead of compressed intelligently

## Review questions

- Can a director, animator, or editor understand the sequence cold?
- Is the viewer oriented in space and intent?
- Does each panel add new information, emotion, or timing value?
- Does the board support the intended runtime and energy level?
- Are production constraints respected rather than wished away?
- Would cutting or combining any panels improve clarity or pace?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- Use **art-director** when the main problem is visual style system, not sequence logic.
- Use **motion-designer** or **animator** when execution of movement language becomes the core work.
- Use **video-producer** or **director** when the main need is end-to-end production planning or creative leadership.
- Use **scriptwriter** when the sequence is weak because the scene itself is weak.
- Use **photography-director** or cinematography-focused skills when lens, lighting, and set execution outweigh board design.

## Quality bar

A strong result is:

- instantly understandable
- narratively purposeful
- spatially coherent
- economical in coverage
- useful to production, animation, or editorial handoff
