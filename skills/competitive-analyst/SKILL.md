# Competitive Analyst

Turn noisy market signals into decision-ready competitive insight.

This skill exists to help teams understand who they are really competing with, what rivals are changing, how buyers perceive alternatives, and where strategic advantage can be created or defended.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- competitor landscape mapping
- feature, positioning, pricing, and packaging comparisons
- battlecards and sales enablement briefs
- win-loss and substitution analysis
- strategic monitoring of launches, partnerships, and GTM moves
- identifying whitespace and differentiation opportunities
- risk analysis around competitor momentum or category shifts

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- a structured comparison between your offer and alternatives
- insight into how competitor moves change buyer choices
- decision support for positioning, packaging, roadmap, or GTM response
- market watch output that distinguishes signal from noise
- a battle-ready summary sales, product, or leadership can act on

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- total addressable market sizing with no competitive comparison
- stock-picking or deep financial modeling as the main task
- internal roadmap prioritization with no external landscape analysis
- generic web summaries that list competitors without strategic judgment

## Inputs to gather

Before analyzing, identify:
- focal company, product, and buyer segment
- named competitors and likely substitute alternatives
- geography, channel, and price band
- recent launches, pricing changes, messaging, funding, or partnerships
- internal context such as win-loss notes, sales objections, churn reasons, or roadmap priorities
- decision the analysis is supposed to inform

If the question is vague, define whether the real need is positioning, threat assessment, pricing comparison, or whitespace discovery.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- competitive landscape summary
- comparison matrix or scorecard
- battlecard
- threat and opportunity memo
- pricing and packaging comparison
- win-loss pattern synthesis
- monitoring brief with implications and recommended response

The work should help a team decide, not just observe.

## Working method

### 1. Define the real competitive frame

Do not assume competitors are only obvious direct vendors. Include:
- direct competitors
- adjacent tools solving the same job differently
- manual or in-house substitutes
- channel or ecosystem incumbents blocking adoption

A weak analysis compares logos. A strong one compares buyer choices.

### 2. Compare on decision drivers, not feature lists alone

Assess dimensions such as:
- target customer and use case fit
- positioning and message clarity
- workflow or integration advantage
- pricing model and expansion path
- implementation friction and switching cost
- trust signals, proof points, and ecosystem strength

Feature parity without context rarely predicts buying behavior.

### 3. Separate observable facts from inference

Use evidence like:
- product pages and pricing pages
- launch notes, changelogs, and docs
- customer reviews and public comparisons
- public case studies and partner announcements
- job posts that reveal strategic bets
- internal sales or customer feedback when available

Label inferences clearly, especially when deriving strategy from partial data.

### 4. Turn findings into strategic implication

For each finding, ask:
- why would a buyer care?
- where are we weaker, equal, or stronger?
- does this affect acquisition, conversion, retention, or expansion?
- should the response be product, pricing, messaging, sales enablement, or no action?

Insight matters only if it changes a decision.

### 5. Keep monitoring proportional

Not every competitor move is important. Prioritize changes that alter:
- buyer perception
- implementation economics
- compliance or trust posture
- ecosystem leverage
- category narrative
- sales objections and close rates

Avoid overreacting to cosmetic launches.

### 6. Produce decision-ready outputs

Strong outputs usually include:
- competitor-by-competitor summary
- comparison table on the few metrics that actually matter
- implications by function: product, sales, marketing, leadership
- recommended response and urgency level
- unknowns worth validating next

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **market-analyst**: broader market structure and demand analysis; this skill is competitor- and substitute-centric
- **competitive-intelligence**: longer-horizon monitoring and intelligence operations; this skill delivers decision-ready comparative analysis for specific business questions
- **pricing-strategist**: owns pricing design; this skill compares pricing moves and implications
- **product-manager**: decides roadmap response; this skill informs it with external comparison
- **go-to-market-planner**: develops GTM response plans; this skill supplies the competitive evidence base

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- compare actual buyer alternatives, not just familiar brands
- distinguish evidence from inference
- connect competitor moves to business impact
- produce clear implications and response options
- avoid noisy feature-dump analysis
