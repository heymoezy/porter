# Market Analyst

Turn vague “how big is this market?” questions into strategy-grade answers. Good market analysis defines the market carefully, sizes it with visible assumptions, explains what drives demand and value capture, and ends with a decision about where to play.

## Core workflow

1. **Anchor the decision**
   - State the business decision: enter, expand, prioritize, price, position, or raise.
   - Define the time horizon, geography, customer type, and business model.
   - Say what the analysis must help decide, not just what it should describe.

2. **Define the market before sizing it**
   - Specify inclusions, exclusions, substitutes, and adjacent categories.
   - Name the unit of analysis: spend, customers, seats, transactions, volume, or capacity.
   - Distinguish category size from reachable market and from realistic share.
   - Reject fuzzy categories that make the TAM look impressive but useless.

3. **Size from multiple angles**
   - Use top-down anchors when credible external category data exists.
   - Use bottom-up logic when buyer counts, usage, and pricing are knowable.
   - Use value-chain logic when spend and margin pool sit in different layers.
   - If methods conflict, explain why; do not average incompatible models.

4. **Segment only where the answer changes**
   - Segment by variables that alter economics or win conditions: customer size, use case, industry, geography, channel, compliance burden, buyer urgency, or willingness to pay.
   - Avoid decorative segmentation that creates slides without decisions.

5. **Map competition at the job level**
   - Include direct competitors, adjacent substitutes, in-house workarounds, and non-consumption.
   - Explain how each option wins: distribution, price, product depth, trust, switching cost, ecosystem, regulation, or habit.
   - Separate “popular” from “dangerous.” The scariest substitute is often not the loudest brand.

6. **Separate growth from attractiveness**
   - Growth rate alone is not enough.
   - Assess margin structure, CAC/channel power, concentration risk, compliance drag, platform dependence, switching behavior, and barriers to scale.
   - Show where value accrues and who keeps it.

7. **End with a where-to-play answer**
   - Recommend the segment, geography, buyer, or wedge to prioritize.
   - State why now, what evidence supports it, and what assumptions could break the call.
   - Name the next validation step if confidence is incomplete.

## Default output structure

1. Decision and scope
2. Market definition and exclusions
3. Sizing method and assumption table
4. Segment attractiveness comparison
5. Competitive and substitute landscape
6. Demand drivers and structural risks
7. Recommendation, confidence, and next validation step

## Evidence rules

- Separate **fact**, **estimate**, and **interpretation**.
- Prefer ranges over fake precision.
- Label stale, sparse, or conflicting source data.
- Make sensitivity visible when a few assumptions dominate the outcome.
- If the market cannot be sized honestly, say so and give a bounded directional view instead.

## Use this skill for

- TAM / SAM / SOM analysis
- market-entry or adjacency evaluation
- category structure and trend analysis
- competitive landscape and substitute mapping
- segment prioritization
- investor or leadership market narratives grounded in evidence

## Do not use this skill for

- running interviews, surveys, or desk-research plans as the main task
- product usability or UX behavior studies
- valuation of a single company
- unsupported “this market is hot” commentary

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **market-researcher**: designs and synthesizes primary/secondary evidence collection; use this skill when the core task is market structure, sizing, and strategic interpretation.
- **pricing-strategist**: designs monetization and packaging; use this skill when pricing is only part of market context.
- **financial-analyst**: models company financial outcomes; use this skill when the question is external market reality.
- **go-to-market-planner**: turns market choice into launch and channel action; use this skill after the market call is made.

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- defines the market tightly
- shows all material assumptions
- avoids inflated TAM theater
- distinguishes market growth from value capture
- compares segments in decision terms
- ends with a clear strategic call or explicit uncertainty boundary
