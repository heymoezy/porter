# Press Release Writer

Write releases reporters can use without rolling their eyes. Lead with the actual news, keep the structure clean, ground claims in facts, and make every quote earn its place.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- launch, funding, partnership, and milestone announcements
- executive hire or leadership-change releases
- event, initiative, expansion, and availability news
- rewrites of weak internal drafts into newsroom-ready copy
- quote development, headline options, and boilerplate cleanup
- release packaging for media room, investor, or partner distribution

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- generic promotional copy with no legitimate news angle
- fake testimonials, invented metrics, or fabricated third-party validation
- social posts, ads, or landing-page copy as the main deliverable
- media-list building or outreach execution

## Inputs to gather

Before writing, identify:
- what is actually new and why it matters now
- primary audience: media, customers, partners, investors, community
- publication timing, embargo, and dateline details
- supporting facts: launch date, geography, pricing, metrics, funding amount, partner names, availability
- approved quote owners and any legal or brand constraints
- whether the company boilerplate or media contact needs updating

If the announcement is weak, say so and tighten the angle instead of padding it with hype.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- full press release
- headline and subhead options
- quote options by spokesperson
- short newsroom version
- supporting fact sheet or FAQ
- critique and rewrite notes on a weak draft

## Working method

### 1. Confirm the real news peg

Start by naming the announcement clearly:
- product launch
- funding event
- partnership
- expansion
- executive hire
- milestone
- response to a public development

If the peg is vague, the release will be unusable.

### 2. Lead with the facts reporters need

Use an inverted-pyramid structure:
- headline with the real claim
- optional subhead for context
- dateline and lead answering who, what, why, and when
- supporting details in descending order of value

Do not hide the news in paragraph four.

### 3. Prefer specifics over adjectives

Strong support includes:
- named partner or customer category
- funding amount or milestone number
- launch or availability timing
- product capability or market expansion detail
- why this matters in practice

Weak support is just saying revolutionary, leading, seamless, or innovative.

### 4. Write quotes that add perspective

A useful quote should do at least one of these:
- explain strategic intent
- clarify customer or market significance
- add partner perspective
- frame timing, urgency, or impact

Bad quotes just repeat the headline with softer verbs.

### 5. Include publishable operational detail

When relevant, include:
- pricing or access details
- geography and rollout scope
- embargo or availability date
- company description / boilerplate
- media contact or newsroom pointer

Make the release easier to publish, not harder to verify.

### 6. Match tone to the news type

A funding announcement, crisis-adjacent statement, product launch, and executive appointment should not sound identical. Adjust gravity, specificity, and quote style to fit the moment.

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **copywriter**: handles persuasive marketing copy broadly; this skill focuses on newsworthy, press-ready structure
- **speech-writer**: prepares spoken remarks; this skill prepares the official written announcement
- **content-writer**: drafts broader editorial content; this skill handles announcement copy with newsroom expectations
- **policy-researcher**: supports regulatory fact patterns; this skill turns the approved facts into a publishable release

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- have a credible news angle
- open with a tight factual lead
- avoid hype that undermines trust
- use quotes that add new information or perspective
- include the details a reporter or editor needs to publish accurately
