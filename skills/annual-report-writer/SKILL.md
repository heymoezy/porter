# Annual Report Writer

Build annual reports that are credible, structured, and genuinely useful to stakeholders. The job is to turn a year of activity into a coherent, evidence-backed narrative that explains performance, context, priorities, risks, and next steps without drifting into empty corporate language.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- annual report structure and planning
- executive letter drafting
- year-in-review narrative writing
- KPI/impact summary writing
- governance and strategy sections
- stakeholder-facing performance summaries
- nonprofit impact reporting
- integrated narrative that connects data, milestones, risks, and future direction

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- a full annual report or annual review document
- synthesis of operational, financial, strategic, and impact information
- clean narrative structure for investors, board members, donors, customers, staff, or partners
- executive-summary writing grounded in real performance
- transformation of scattered yearly updates into one coherent report

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- audited financial statement preparation
- legal/regulatory filing advice beyond narrative drafting
- generic marketing copy with no annual-report structure
- a quarterly update unless the format intentionally mirrors annual reporting

## Inputs to gather

Before writing, identify:
- organization type: public company, private company, startup, nonprofit, foundation, internal business unit
- primary audience: shareholders, donors, board, staff, customers, regulators, partners
- reporting requirements or mandatory sections
- year’s major events, metrics, wins, setbacks, and risks
- financial context and any approved figures
- leadership messages and strategic priorities
- brand voice and tone expectations

If numbers or claims are not verified, flag them rather than smoothing over gaps.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- annual report outline
- section-by-section draft
- CEO/executive letter
- performance and KPI narrative
- impact summary
- governance/risk summary
- editorial recommendations for structure and clarity
- report messaging hierarchy

Use headings and section architecture clearly. Tie narrative claims to evidence.

## Working method

### 1. Start with audience and purpose

An annual report can serve different purposes:
- investor confidence and strategic clarity
- board oversight and governance transparency
- donor trust and impact communication
- internal alignment and accountability
- public-facing reputation and legitimacy

The report must be written for the primary reader, not as a generic corporate artifact.

### 2. Build the report around the year’s real story

A strong annual report usually answers:
- what changed this year?
- what mattered most?
- what improved, what slipped, and why?
- how does the organization measure progress?
- what risks and constraints shaped the year?
- what comes next?

Do not build the report as a pile of sections with no narrative spine.

### 3. Balance narrative and evidence

Each major claim should be supported by:
- KPI movement
- financial context
- examples or initiatives
- customer/beneficiary/market signals
- operational outcomes

Avoid statements like “we had a transformative year” unless the report proves it.

### 4. Structure the report cleanly

Common section architecture may include:
- executive/CEO/chair letter
- year in review
- strategy and priorities
- operating highlights or business performance
- KPI/impact snapshot
- financial overview
- governance / leadership / risk
- outlook or next-year priorities

Tailor the exact structure to the organization type.

### 5. Write honestly about tradeoffs and setbacks

A credible report acknowledges:
- missed targets
- external headwinds
- strategic shifts
- lessons learned
- areas still in progress

Stakeholder trust rises when the report sounds real, not airbrushed.

### 6. Keep the language clear and executive-readable

Strong annual report writing should be:
- concise
- evidence-backed
- specific
- low-jargon where possible
- internally consistent across sections
- strong enough to quote or excerpt elsewhere

### 7. Make sections work independently and together

Readers often skim only parts of the report.
Each section should stand alone, while still reinforcing the overall story.
That means:
- no contradictory messages across sections
- no repeated empty filler
- metrics used consistently
- narrative progression from reflection → explanation → outlook

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **financial-analyst**: may interpret financial performance in depth; this skill writes the stakeholder-facing annual narrative
- **technical-writer / documentation-writer**: structure and clarity overlap, but this skill is specialized for annual-report conventions and stakeholder messaging
- **copywriter**: more campaign/persuasion oriented; this skill emphasizes credibility, structure, and year-in-review synthesis
- **proposal-writer**: future-facing persuasion; this skill is retrospective and accountability-oriented

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- reflect the organization’s actual year, not generic corporate filler
- align structure to audience and purpose
- make metrics and impact readable
- acknowledge challenges honestly
- connect performance to strategy and future direction
- be clean enough to guide design/layout downstream
