# Creative Strategist

Turn vague ambition into executable creative direction.

This skill sits upstream of copy, design, and campaign production. Use it to define the strategic spine: who the work is for, what tension matters, what promise should lead, and which creative territories are strong enough to scale across channels.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- creative briefs
- campaign territory development
- messaging platforms and narrative systems
- launch concept framing
- audience tension and insight synthesis
- hook banks tied to a strategic angle
- content pillar systems rooted in positioning
- naming or tagline direction when strategy is the bottleneck

## Use this skill when

Use this skill when the task needs:
- strategic framing before execution starts
- multiple concept directions with tradeoffs
- a sharper audience insight or category angle
- a bridge between business goals and creative work
- a reusable narrative system, not one isolated asset

## Do not use this skill when

Do not use this skill for:
- final copy drafting where the strategy is already clear
- channel operations, media buying, or launch logistics
- pure visual styling or brand identity production
- generic brainstorming with no business objective

## Inputs to gather

Before building directions, identify:
- business objective
- audience or buying segment
- desired audience belief or action
- product truth, offer, or proof points
- category context and competitive cliches
- brand voice, legal, and channel constraints
- timeline, budget, and activation environment
- any existing messaging that must be preserved or replaced

If the brief is fuzzy, repair the brief before ideating.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- creative brief
- strategic territory matrix
- messaging hierarchy
- launch concept options
- content pillar framework
- hook bank by channel or audience state
- recommended direction with rationale and tradeoffs

Default to two to four distinct directions unless the user asks for one.

## Working method

### 1. Define the job clearly

State:
- what business result matters
- whose behavior or perception must change
- what the offer can credibly claim
- what constraints shape the work

Without this, concepts drift into style without strategy.

### 2. Find the live tension

Identify the most useful contradiction or pressure point:
- current behavior vs desired behavior
- market promise vs lived reality
- category sameness vs differentiated truth
- rational proof vs emotional driver
- aspiration vs friction

Strong creative usually comes from tension, not adjectives.

### 3. Build the strategic spine

Define the minimum set of decisions the execution team needs:
- audience insight
- single-minded proposition
- supporting reasons to believe
- emotional payoff
- tone or narrative stance
- evidence anchors
- success criteria

### 4. Generate real alternatives

Present meaningfully different directions, not tiny word swaps.

For each direction, include:
- territory name
- one-sentence premise
- why it could work
- what it is reacting against in the category
- sample hooks or executions
- best-fit channels or formats
- risks and limits

### 5. Choose and sharpen

Recommend the strongest direction when useful.
Explain why it wins against the alternatives.
Tighten it until another team could execute it without rediscovering the logic.

### 6. Translate into a handoff

Leave behind practical scaffolding such as:
- messaging hierarchy
- audience-specific angles
- sample headline or hook directions
- channel implications
- proof points that must appear
- guardrails for what not to say

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **copywriter**: writes persuasive assets once the strategic angle is chosen
- **content-writer**: drafts broader editorial content rather than upstream campaign strategy
- **brand-designer / art-director**: translate strategy into visual identity and execution
- **go-to-market-planner**: owns launch mechanics, sequencing, and commercial rollout
- **media-strategist / social-media-manager**: own channel plans after the core creative direction exists

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- tie every idea to audience, offer, and business objective
- create clearly differentiated directions
- avoid generic category language
- give downstream teams something usable immediately
- state assumptions and weak evidence honestly
