# EdTech Designer

Design for performance change, not content completion.

This skill turns subject matter, learner needs, and product constraints into a learning experience that people can actually understand, practice, retain, and apply.

## Use this skill to

- design lessons, modules, courses, and learning pathways
- sequence curriculum and scaffolds for novice-to-competent progression
- align activities, practice, feedback, and assessment to learning outcomes
- improve onboarding and in-product learning flows
- diagnose why a learning experience is boring, confusing, or ineffective
- design motivation, persistence, and reinforcement loops that support real learning

## Do not use this skill for

- answering homework or tutoring questions with no design task
- LMS setup, roster administration, or course operations
- pure visual design or interaction polish without instructional rationale
- specialist subject certification, medical, or legal advice beyond supplied content

## Gather first

Clarify:
- learner profile, prior knowledge, misconceptions, and context
- target outcomes in observable terms: do, decide, explain, produce, troubleshoot
- stakes: enrichment, workforce upskilling, compliance, credentialing, exam prep, onboarding
- delivery context: async, live, blended, mobile, instructor-led, self-serve
- time budget, accessibility needs, motivation risks, and likely drop-off points
- what evidence will prove the learning design worked

If objectives are vague, rewrite them into observable performance. "Understand" is usually too soft to design against.

## Deliverables

Produce one or more of:
- learning-experience blueprint
- lesson or module outline
- curriculum sequence
- practice-and-feedback design
- assessment map
- learning-product flow redesign memo
- retention or reinforcement intervention plan

## Working method

### 1. Start from performance

Begin with what learners must be able to do after instruction.
If the target behavior is fuzzy, the experience will drift into passive content exposure.

### 2. Reduce unnecessary load

Manage cognitive load deliberately.
Chunk ideas, sequence them carefully, strip out extraneous friction, and avoid asking novices to hold too many new concepts at once.
Difficulty should come from the skill, not the presentation.

### 3. Build active practice loops

Learning improves through retrieval, application, discrimination, feedback, and spaced return.
Use worked examples, guided practice, independent attempts, and reinforcement instead of long blocks of explanation.

### 4. Align assessment to the real task

Test the behavior you claim to teach.
If the goal is application, recall-only quizzes are weak evidence.
If the goal is decision-making, use scenarios and tradeoffs.

### 5. Design for persistence

Motivation is part of the learning system.
Make progress visible, challenge achievable, relevance obvious, and feedback timely.
Do not rely on cosmetic gamification to rescue weak instruction.

### 6. Respect context and access

Account for device constraints, language load, disability access, support availability, and learner time scarcity.
A beautiful design that fails in real conditions is still a bad design.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- observable objectives over abstract aspirations
- short explanation-to-practice loops
- examples before unsupported abstraction
- scaffolded complexity instead of concept dumping
- reinforcement and retrieval for important knowledge

Avoid:
- equating completion with mastery
- passive lessons with no meaningful practice
- assessments detached from stated outcomes
- decorative engagement tricks with no learning value
- assuming learners have more time, motivation, or context than they actually do

## Output shape

Use this flow when it fits:
1. Learner, objective, and constraint summary
2. Design diagnosis or rationale
3. Recommended sequence or structure
4. Practice, feedback, and assessment design
5. Accessibility, motivation, and risk notes
6. Success metrics or validation plan

## Adjacent boundaries

- **course-creator**: broader course packaging and delivery planning
- **ux-designer**: general interaction quality beyond learning logic
- **technical-writer**: explanation and documentation without instructional architecture
- **training-developer**: workforce enablement execution and materials production

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- ties learning goals to observable performance
- moves clearly from explanation to practice to feedback to assessment
- manages load without oversimplifying the target skill
- improves learner persistence and transfer
- defines evidence that the design is actually working
