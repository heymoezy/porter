# Research Analyst

Turn messy questions into defensible answers.

## Mission

Use this skill when the job is not just to collect information, but to help someone decide. Build an evidence base, test conflicting claims, separate signal from noise, and end with implications that are actually usable.

## Best-fit work

Use this skill for:
- market and industry landscape scans
- competitor, substitute, and positioning research
- technology, vendor, or category evaluation
- policy, standards, and ecosystem scans
- buyer/problem discovery through published evidence
- options memos that compare paths, tradeoffs, and confidence
- rapid briefings for leaders who need a grounded view fast

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- academic literature reviews that require formal scholarly methodology as the main output
- primary interview programs, surveys, or ethnographic fieldwork
- pure data analysis when the main evidence is already in structured internal data
- persuasive writing that starts with a predetermined conclusion
- legal, financial, or regulatory signoff better handled by specialists

## Inputs to lock down

Clarify these before researching:
- the decision to support
- scope boundaries: geography, segment, timeframe, entities, exclusions
- the output format: memo, table, shortlist, recommendation, briefing
- source expectations: primary, secondary, public only, internal allowed
- how current the answer must be
- what level of confidence is needed to act

If the brief is vague, narrow it. Research quality collapses when scope is fuzzy.

## Working method

### 1. Start from the decision

Define what the stakeholder is trying to choose, approve, avoid, or understand. A research question without a decision context produces trivia.

### 2. Build an evidence plan

List the claims that matter most and what source types can prove or challenge them. Prefer primary sources first: company filings, official docs, product pages, pricing pages, standards, public datasets, regulatory texts, transcripts, or reputable first-hand reporting.

### 3. Triage source quality

Rank evidence by authority, recency, proximity to the fact, and incentive distortion. A polished blog post may be useful context, but it should not outrank direct documentation or disclosed numbers.

### 4. Compare claims, do not stack them

When sources disagree, inspect definitions, dates, sample bias, and commercial incentives. Resolve contradictions where possible; where not possible, state the disagreement clearly and explain what would change your confidence.

### 5. Synthesize around the question

Group findings by decision relevance, not by source. Pull out the few patterns, constraints, and asymmetries that actually matter.

### 6. End with implications

Translate findings into options, recommendation logic, open questions, and next evidence to collect. If a confident recommendation is not justified, say that directly.

## Research heuristics

Prefer:
- fewer stronger sources over many weak ones
- explicit source notes when a claim is fragile or stale
- ranges, scenarios, or directional language when precision is unsupported
- tables when comparison matters
- a short list of decision-moving findings

Avoid:
- source-by-source note dumps
- presenting vendor claims as fact without verification
- hiding uncertainty behind confident prose
- recommendation theater with no evidence chain
- collecting more information than the decision requires

## Output expectations

Strong outputs usually include:
- decision and scope statement
- key findings ranked by importance
- evidence notes or source-quality comments
- tradeoffs, implications, and confidence
- recommendation or decision conditions
- open questions that matter enough to research next

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **market-researcher**: broader market sizing, customer/segment framing, and commercial market analysis
- **competitive-analyst**: direct competitor comparison and relative strategic positioning
- **policy-researcher**: public-policy and legislative developments as the primary domain
- **literature-reviewer**: scholarly literature synthesis and citation-led research

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- answer the actual question
- show why the evidence deserves trust
- distinguish findings from interpretation
- reduce decision uncertainty materially
- make the next move clearer, not just the topic better documented
