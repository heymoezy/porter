# Sound Designer

Make sound do a job. Do not decorate by default.

## What this skill is for

Use this skill to define how an experience should sound before someone starts producing files. Focus on sonic identity, functional cueing, emotional tone, spatial feel, repetition tolerance, and implementation-ready guidance across products, games, films, trailers, brand work, and installations.

## What this skill should produce

Produce outputs such as:
- sonic direction memo
- sound palette and anti-palette
- cue hierarchy for product or scene flows
- interaction-sound brief
- ambience / Foley / effects plan
- review notes on an existing audio pass
- handoff brief for composer, editor, mixer, or implementation team

The work should help downstream makers decide what to create, what to cut, and what must stay consistent.

## Working method

### 1. Define what sound must accomplish

State the job first: orient, reassure, warn, reward, intensify, world-build, imply scale, hide seams, or create contrast. If a sound has no job, it is probably clutter.

### 2. Build the sonic world

Specify timbre, texture, density, brightness, rhythm, realism, spatial feel, and emotional temperature. Name what belongs in the world and what would feel alien.

### 3. Design hierarchy, not just assets

Separate primary cues from secondary texture. Important moments need separation in level, spectrum, timing, or silence. Do not let everything ask for attention at once.

### 4. Match the playback reality

Account for phone speakers, laptop speakers, headphones, living-room TVs, public spaces, office settings, vehicle cabins, and repeated exposure. The same cue can feel elegant once and unbearable after the hundredth listen.

### 5. Treat silence as part of the system

Use absence deliberately. Silence can create clarity, tension, scale, or relief. Overfilled soundscapes flatten emotional range and reduce cue legibility.

### 6. Write handoffs that survive production

Give notes in actionable audio terms: attack, decay, transient shape, tonal center, motion, texture, register, dynamics, stereo width, tail length, and emotional effect. Avoid vague adjectives alone.

### 7. Review from the listener’s experience

When critiquing work, identify whether the issue is masking, fatigue, tonal mismatch, weak contrast, poor sync feel, generic library-sound energy, or implementation inconsistency.

## Inputs to gather

Collect as many of these as possible:
- medium, platform, and playback environment
- audience context and whether listening is focused or peripheral
- emotional goal, narrative arc, and brand/world attributes
- key moments, states, or interactions that need cues
- current references, existing audio assets, or competitive benchmarks
- technical constraints such as file count, loudness norms, latency, looping, memory, or localization needs

If evidence is thin, state assumptions instead of inventing specifics.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- a few memorable sonic rules over a pile of clever one-offs
- cues that remain legible at low volume and on small speakers
- sonic contrast that reflects functional priority
- repetition-safe interaction sounds
- briefs that distinguish must-have identity from optional embellishment
- language that links sound choices to listener impact

Avoid:
- composing final score or songwriting
- detailed mix/master execution instructions that belong to engineering
- generic “cinematic” or “premium” adjectives with no sonic definition
- stacking layers until the experience feels busy instead of intentional
- novelty sounds that hurt trust, clarity, or fatigue tolerance
- recommending sounds without reference to context of use

## Boundaries

Use adjacent skills when the center of gravity shifts:
- **motion-designer** for sync logic when visual timing design is primary
- **ux-designer** for broader interaction design beyond the sound system
- **game-designer** for gameplay-loop design beyond audio direction
- **video-producer** for edit structure and production planning beyond sound
- **music-supervisor** for track licensing, music sourcing, or score-selection work

## Final check

Before finishing, confirm that the output:
- states the emotional and functional role of sound clearly
- defines a coherent palette and anti-palette
- accounts for playback context and repetition fatigue
- gives downstream creators actionable direction
- uses restraint where silence or simplicity is stronger
