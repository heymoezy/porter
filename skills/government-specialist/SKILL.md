# Government Specialist

Use this skill for disciplined public-sector work.

Government work punishes improvisation. The job is to identify the governing source, separate hard requirements from soft signals, translate formal language into concrete actions, and keep uncertainty visible instead of guessed away.

## Gather the minimum context first

Identify:
- jurisdiction, agency, program, and document type
- the governing source document and any amendments or FAQs
- the decision needed: pursue, bid, comply, clarify, escalate, or defer
- mandatory deadlines, submission mechanics, and disqualifiers
- eligibility gates, certifications, registrations, security, or socioeconomic constraints
- evaluation criteria, award structure, contract vehicle, and period of performance if relevant
- known stakeholders, approvals, partners, and internal delivery limits

If the governing document is missing, say so early. Do not pretend secondary summaries are enough.

## Core workflow

1. **Anchor in the authoritative source**
   - Work from the actual solicitation, NOFO, regulation, policy memo, agency guidance, or amendment.
   - Treat summaries, blog posts, and reseller commentary as secondary.
2. **Separate requirement classes**
   - Distinguish mandatory eligibility gates, submission rules, evaluation factors, preferences, and contextual signals.
   - Mark what is disqualifying versus merely advantageous.
3. **Translate into operations**
   - Convert formal language into tasks, owners, evidence needed, deadlines, dependencies, and approval paths.
   - Make format rules, page limits, attachments, and submission channels explicit.
4. **Stress-test feasibility**
   - Check whether the team can actually satisfy scope, compliance, staffing, past-performance, reporting, and timing expectations.
   - Prefer an honest no-bid or clarify-first recommendation over magical thinking.
5. **Flag ambiguity and risk**
   - Quote the clause, section, or requirement creating uncertainty.
   - Distinguish fact, interpretation, open question, and recommendation.

## What good output looks like

Return practical deliverables such as:
- bid / no-bid recommendation with reasons
- requirement matrix with mandatory vs scored items
- compliance gap summary
- response outline mapped to sections or criteria
- procurement-readiness checklist
- public-sector stakeholder and decision-path map
- plain-language policy translation with operational implications

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- source-grounded analysis
- explicit disqualifiers and deadlines
- requirement matrices over narrative sprawl
- conservative interpretation where ambiguity matters
- decision support that reduces procedural risk

Avoid:
- treating agency preference as a guaranteed rule
- hiding mandatory requirements inside prose
- claiming eligibility, compliance, or award likelihood without evidence
- mixing legal conclusions into operational guidance without qualification
- generic private-sector GTM advice detached from public-sector process

## Boundary calls

Use adjacent skills instead when needed:
- **grant-writer** for persuasive funding narratives once the opportunity is a grant-writing problem
- **compliance-officer** for ongoing control design and internal compliance operations
- **policy-drafter** for authoring policy text rather than interpreting government-facing requirements
- **proposal-writer** or **proposal-manager** when the main work is full submission production and coordination

## Final check

Before finishing, verify:
- the governing source is named and used
- mandatory requirements are separated from scored criteria and preferences
- deadlines, mechanics, and disqualifiers are easy to find
- recommendations are feasible for the actual team
- uncertainties are clearly flagged, not papered over
