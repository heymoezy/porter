# Proposal Writer

Write proposals that make approval easier.

## Use this skill to
- draft client proposals, scopes of work, and commercial offers
- write internal funding or initiative proposals
- shape partnership, pilot, or grant proposals
- turn discovery notes into a decision-ready document
- improve an existing proposal’s logic, credibility, and close

## Do not use this skill to
- draft the legal contract that follows approval
- write brand copy, landing pages, or capability brochures
- describe technical implementation in exhaustive engineering detail when the real job is executive approval
- hide uncertainty, dependencies, or exclusions to make the proposal sound stronger

## Gather first
- decision-maker, buyer, and approval path
- problem, opportunity, or mandate being addressed
- stakes, desired outcomes, and evaluation criteria
- proposed scope, deliverables, owners, and timeline
- pricing, budget, resources, or funding ask
- assumptions, dependencies, exclusions, and proof points

## Deliverables that fit this skill
- full proposal draft
- executive summary
- scope / deliverables table
- pricing or investment rationale
- options comparison
- assumptions, risk, and exclusion section
- acceptance / next-step close

## Working method

### 1. Write to the decision, not to the document type
Ask what must happen next:
- approval
- budget release
- shortlist selection
- pilot go-ahead
- partnership commitment

A proposal that does not change a decision is just formatted optimism.

### 2. Lead with stakes and value
Make the opening answer:
- what problem or opportunity matters
- why it matters now
- what outcome is being proposed
- why this option is credible

Do not open with company biography unless the buyer truly needs it.

### 3. Make the offer concrete
Define clearly:
- what is included
- what is excluded
- deliverables and milestones
- roles and dependencies
- decision points and acceptance conditions

Vague scope kills trust and pricing confidence.

### 4. Connect money to logic
Whether the ask is price, budget, headcount, or grant funding, explain:
- what the investment covers
- why it is sized that way
- what options or tradeoffs exist
- what assumptions affect cost or timing

Do not float a number without the structure behind it.

### 5. Handle risk without weakening the proposal
Name:
- dependencies
- client or stakeholder responsibilities
- risks and mitigations
- excluded scenarios
- what would trigger repricing, rescoping, or escalation

Credibility beats bravado.

### 6. Write for scanability first
Use headings, bullets, tables, and short paragraphs. Decision-makers often skim in this order:
1. executive summary
2. recommendation
3. scope
4. timeline
5. price or ask
6. risk / assumptions
7. next step

### 7. Close with a specific action
End with the exact move you want:
- approve
- sign off
- schedule workshop
- release budget
- confirm pilot
- select option A/B/C

Do not end with vague availability language.

## Adjacent skill boundaries
- **copywriter**: writes persuasive marketing copy; this skill writes approval-oriented decision documents
- **contract-reviewer**: reviews legal language; this skill shapes the proposal before contracting
- **grant-writer**: specializes in funder-fit and grant requirements; this skill handles the broader proposal craft when grant specialization is not the main challenge
- **project-architect**: structures delivery work; this skill packages that work into an approvable offer

## Quality bar
A strong result should:
- make the decision-maker’s problem and proposed outcome explicit
- connect scope, timeline, and pricing into one coherent offer
- surface assumptions, exclusions, and risks honestly
- feel credible, specific, and easy to approve
- end with a concrete next-step close
