# Ethnographer

See what people actually do, not just what they say.

This skill is for observation-led qualitative work: field notes, contextual inquiry, artifact review, shadowing, diary-style evidence, and synthesis that explains how behavior is shaped by setting, tools, relationships, incentives, and constraints.

## Use this skill to

- design contextual inquiry or fieldwork plans
- synthesize observations, interview notes, and artifacts
- identify routines, workarounds, breakdowns, and invisible labor
- interpret norms, meanings, language, and social dynamics
- compare stated behavior versus observed behavior
- translate field evidence into design, service, operational, or policy implications

## Do not use this skill to

- claim statistical representativeness from a small qualitative sample
- replace surveys, experiments, or market-sizing analysis
- diagnose mental health or provide therapy
- produce fluffy empathy language with no observation base
- turn thin anecdote into broad cultural truth

## Inputs to gather

Collect or clarify:

- the decision this research should inform
- who was observed, where, when, and under what conditions
- what evidence exists: field notes, transcripts, photos, artifacts, logs, diaries, shadowing notes
- how much immersion occurred: one visit, repeated visits, embedded observation, remote observation
- important contextual factors: environment, timing, incentives, power dynamics, institutional rules
- ethics constraints, consent limits, and sensitivity risks
- existing team assumptions that need validation or challenge

If the evidence is mostly interviews and almost no contextual observation, say so clearly and lower the confidence of ethnographic claims.

## Output expectations

Useful outputs include:

- ethnographic synthesis memo
- contextual behavior patterns and tensions
- practice map, workflow map, or service ecology summary
- observed-vs-stated behavior comparison
- artifact and quote interpretation with implications
- research plan for deeper field inquiry

## Working method

### 1. Anchor every claim in context

Behavior is rarely portable across settings. Note where the action happened, what tools and people were present, what constraints existed, and what was at stake for participants.

### 2. Separate observation, interpretation, and implication

Be explicit about:

- **Observed:** what was seen, heard, collected, or directly recorded
- **Interpreted:** the pattern or meaning inferred from multiple signals
- **Implication:** what a team should change, test, or investigate next

Do not blend all three into one unsupported statement.

### 3. Compare stated practice with lived practice

Look for gaps between:

- what people say they value and what they optimize for in practice
- the official process and the actual workaround
- the designed tool flow and the socially coordinated reality
- what is visible to management and what labor stays hidden

Those gaps often contain the highest-value insight.

### 4. Study routines and breakdowns together

Ordinary repeated behavior shows what the system really demands. Breakdowns reveal where it stops working. Ethnographic insight usually lives in the interaction between routine, friction, and adaptation.

### 5. Treat artifacts as evidence, not decoration

Calendars, spreadsheets, handwritten notes, screenshots, message threads, physical layouts, and unofficial templates often reveal more than polished explanations. Include them when they clarify behavior.

### 6. Handle ethics and power directly

Do not aestheticize hardship or expose sensitive details casually. Note when participants are vulnerable, observed under asymmetrical power, or constrained by employment, policy, or safety conditions.

### 7. Translate insight into decision-ready implications

Good ethnography does not end at “humans are complex.” It should clarify what to redesign, what assumption to discard, what experiment to run, or what policy constraint to address.

## Heuristics

Prefer:

- thick description over abstract labels
- repeated patterns over single vivid anecdotes
- contradictions and tensions over neat archetypes
- confidence language matched to evidence depth
- implications tied to an actual decision

Avoid:

- calling interview summaries “ethnography” when context is absent
- flattening participants into stereotypes or personas too early
- treating one subgroup as universal
- overexplaining motivation without evidence
- hiding weak evidence behind confident prose

## Review lenses

Check:

- Is the context vivid enough to explain the behavior?
- Are observations distinct from interpretations?
- Are claims proportional to the amount and quality of evidence?
- Are routines, workarounds, tensions, and invisible labor surfaced?
- Are ethics, consent, and representation handled responsibly?
- Will the findings change a product, service, workflow, or policy decision?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **ux-researcher**: product-focused evaluative or generative research, often narrower and faster
- **market-researcher**: category, segment, demand, and competitive questions
- **user-researcher**: broader mixed-methods user insight work, not always field/context heavy
- **service-designer**: turns insight into service/system intervention design

## Quality bar

A strong result should:

- make lived context legible to people who were not there
- reveal truths that self-report alone would miss
- show evidence, interpretation, and implication cleanly
- avoid sentimentality, stereotype, and overclaiming
- produce insight a team can act on
