# Escalation Handler

Restore control fast. Communicate and resolve in parallel.

This skill is for situations where a normal issue has become a high-stakes one: angry customer, unhappy executive, repeated service failure, broken promise, launch-blocking problem, or messy internal escalation. The core job is not just to answer. It is to stabilize the situation, make ownership obvious, protect credibility, and move toward real resolution.

## Operating principles

- Establish owner, severity, and next update time immediately.
- Separate known facts, unknowns, and current actions.
- Acknowledge impact before explaining mechanics.
- Never promise beyond what the team can actually deliver.
- Keep internal coordination and external communication aligned.
- Close the loop with prevention, not just apology.

## Best-fit tasks

Use this skill for:
- customer or partner escalations with trust, revenue, or deadline risk
- executive-attention support or product issues
- repeated unresolved issues now requiring visible ownership
- cross-functional internal escalations where teams need a shared plan
- service failures needing careful status communication and triage
- de-escalation messaging, action matrices, and trust-repair planning

## Do not use for

Do not use this skill for:
- routine low-severity support replies
- formal legal admissions, settlement language, or privileged crisis-legal work
- public relations statements or media responses requiring communications approval
- deep technical remediation work where the task is fixing the system rather than handling the escalation

## Inputs to collect

Gather or state as unknown:
- what happened, when, and why it escalated
- who is affected and what harm or risk they are experiencing
- severity, business impact, deadline pressure, and reputational exposure
- current status, owners, blockers, and known unknowns
- prior promises made, missed commitments, and emotional temperature
- what authority exists for concessions, timelines, and escalation paths

If facts are incomplete, explicitly state what is confirmed, what is being checked, who owns the check, and when the next update will land.

## Workflow

### 1. Define severity and owner

Name:
- severity / impact level
- coordinating owner
- primary functional owners
- next update time

Confusion about ownership is itself part of the escalation.

### 2. Split the work into three tracks

Run these in parallel:
- **containment:** immediate steps to stop further harm
- **communication:** internal and external messaging cadence
- **resolution:** diagnosis, fix path, or service recovery plan

Do not make stakeholders wait for full root cause before showing control.

### 3. Calibrate the message to the actual harm

Different escalations need different communication:
- repeated support failure → acknowledge trust loss and what changes now
- outage / launch blocker → give status, mitigation, and update cadence
- executive escalation → concise summary, impact, owners, and decisions needed
- partner issue → protect coordination, deadlines, and fallback options

Generic apology language is rarely enough when stakes are high.

### 4. Reduce ambiguity aggressively

Every communication should clarify:
- what is known
- what is not yet known
- what is happening now
- who owns each action
- when the next update will arrive
- what can and cannot be promised yet

### 5. Protect credibility

Credibility drops when teams:
- speculate to sound confident
- miss self-imposed update times
- let multiple owners send conflicting messages
- overuse soothing language without concrete action
- treat a repeat failure like a fresh surprise

### 6. Confirm resolution and learn

After stabilization:
- verify the issue is actually resolved
- confirm the escalated party agrees the immediate concern is addressed
- document root cause if known
- assign prevention actions, owners, and due dates

## Writing heuristics

Prefer:
- calm, accountable tone
- empathy tied to action
- concise status language under pressure
- explicit timing and ownership
- messages that reduce stakeholder uncertainty quickly

Avoid:
- defensiveness before acknowledgement
- “we are looking into it” with no timeline
- hidden uncertainty dressed up as certainty
- too many voices with no coordinator
- declaring success before verification

## Deliverables

Useful outputs include:
- escalation summary for internal stakeholders
- customer / partner / executive response draft
- owner-action matrix
- update cadence recommendation
- de-escalation and trust-repair plan
- post-escalation review notes with prevention steps

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **customer-support**: use for standard issue handling that has not escalated materially
- **customer-success-manager**: use when the main challenge is account strategy and relationship continuity beyond the immediate escalation window
- **disaster-recovery**: use when the core work is service restoration planning and business continuity design
- **incident-responder**: use when technical response execution is primary and stakeholder handling is secondary
- **communications-manager**: use for broader external, public, or executive communications strategy beyond the immediate escalation package

## Quality bar

A strong result:
- makes the situation feel more controlled quickly
- defines ownership, timing, and next actions unambiguously
- acknowledges impact without speculation or overpromising
- helps internal teams execute and external stakeholders stay informed
- leaves behind prevention learning, not just a closed ticket
