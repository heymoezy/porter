# Avatar Art Director

Turn role, personality, and context into a repeatable visual identity for avatars.

This skill is for directing profile imagery and character-presence systems: how a persona should look, what signals it should project immediately, what traits should stay stable across variations, and how to keep small-format identity art expressive without becoming noisy.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- avatar direction for agents, assistants, bots, creators, products, teams, or communities
- translating persona traits into visual identity cues
- defining style systems for profile images and character sets
- creative briefs for illustrators or pixel artists producing avatars
- critique and refinement of avatar concepts
- maintaining consistency across multiple role-based avatars
- direction for mascot-like or symbolic character identities

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- broad brand strategy that extends far beyond avatar identity
- finished illustration production with no direction component
- logo-only exploration
- UI implementation of profile systems
- unconstrained character worldbuilding disconnected from the use context

## Inputs to gather

Before directing, identify:
- who or what the avatar represents
- the personality traits it must signal quickly
- where it will appear: chat lists, forums, dashboards, social, docs, games
- required style constraints: pixel, flat, painterly, mascot, realistic, minimal, 3D-like
- accessibility and small-size readability requirements
- whether it belongs to a family of related avatars
- taboo signals or clichés to avoid
- any brand colors, symbols, uniforms, props, or motifs that matter

If the persona is incoherent, fix the persona brief first. A muddy character produces muddy visual identity.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- avatar direction memo
- visual trait map
- style-system guidance
- concept options with recommendation
- critique and revision notes
- brief for the illustrator or generator producing the art
- rules for keeping a set of avatars consistent

## Working method

### 1. Reduce the persona to a few dominant signals

Pick the signals that must land first:
- trustworthy
- mischievous
- technical
- protective
- elegant
- playful
- disciplined
- uncanny

Not every trait deserves equal weight. Prioritize what should be visible at thumbnail size.

### 2. Translate traits into form

Map personality into concrete visual decisions:
- silhouette and head shape
- expression and gaze
- palette warmth or coolness
- line weight and texture
- clothing, accessories, tools, or symbolic props
- level of realism vs stylization
- geometric vs organic forms

The direction should explain why each trait appears visually, not just list adjectives.

### 3. Design for tiny sizes first

Most avatars are consumed small.
Check whether the identity survives in:
- 24–48 px circles
- compressed lists
- dark and light backgrounds
- monochrome or low-saturation environments

If the idea relies on tiny details, it will fail in use.

### 4. Build consistency rules for families

When multiple avatars belong together, define shared rules for:
- framing and crop
- style language
- lighting logic
- eye treatment and expression range
- accessory usage
- background treatment
- accent-color logic

Variation should communicate role differences without breaking family resemblance.

### 5. Critique for signal clarity, not just polish

When reviewing concepts, ask:
- what does this avatar communicate in one second?
- does it read as intended or drift into another archetype?
- is the expression too generic, too aggressive, too cute, too corporate?
- what details matter at actual size, and what should be removed?

Do not confuse rendered detail with identity strength.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- one memorable identifying trait over many weak details
- silhouettes and expressions that read at small sizes
- props or symbols that reinforce role without clutter
- style systems that allow many avatars to coexist cleanly
- personality cues tied to actual use context

Avoid:
- overdesigned avatars that collapse at thumbnail scale
- generic “friendly robot” defaults when the persona is more specific
- mixing too many art styles in the same set
- accessories with no meaning
- identity systems that depend on background scenes instead of the character itself

## Review lenses

When evaluating avatar direction, check:
- Is the persona legible immediately?
- Will it remain recognizable at small sizes?
- Does it balance expressiveness with simplicity?
- If part of a family, does it feel related without becoming repetitive?
- Are any visual cues likely to be misread culturally or functionally?
- Can another artist execute the direction consistently?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **art-director**: broader campaign or brand visual direction beyond avatar identity
- **brand-designer**: larger identity systems and brand assets
- **illustration-artist / 3d-artist / icon-designer**: execute the directed asset in their medium
- **character-specific creative skills**: expand worldbuilding or narrative beyond avatar scope

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- turn persona into concrete visual rules
- optimize for recognition at actual display size
- define both stable traits and allowed variation
- avoid generic archetypes when stronger specificity is possible
- give downstream makers enough guidance to execute consistently
