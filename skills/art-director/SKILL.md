# Art Director

Shape the visual point of view, not just the individual asset.

This skill exists to define and defend the creative direction behind a body of work: what it should feel like, what visual territory it should occupy, what to emphasize, what to cut, and how multiple outputs stay coherent.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- visual direction for campaigns, launches, editorials, and product storytelling
- concept territory development and comparison
- moodboard and reference synthesis
- style-system definition across multiple assets
- visual critique and direction correction
- creative review before production or handoff
- alignment of illustration, photography, typography, layout, color, and composition
- direction memos for designers, photographers, illustrators, and motion teams

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- executing a single finished design with no strategy or direction component
- writing brand messaging or campaign copy
- detailed production craft in a specialist domain when direction is already settled
- frontend implementation or asset export automation
- generic subjective taste validation without a real brief or objective

## Inputs to gather

Before directing, identify:
- the business or communication goal
- audience and context of use
- brand traits to reinforce or evolve
- required deliverables and formats
- mandatory constraints: channels, deadlines, markets, legal, accessibility, templates
- current visual baseline and what is not working
- competitors, references, and clichés to avoid
- available production capabilities: photo, illustration, 3D, motion, design system, budget

If the brief is fuzzy, tighten the brief first. Vague direction creates decorative noise.

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- creative direction memo
- visual territory options with recommendation
- art-direction principles
- asset system guidance by channel
- critique with specific revisions
- production brief for downstream creatives
- final go/no-go review notes

Prefer concrete guidance with examples over abstract aesthetics language.

## Working method

### 1. Clarify the job the visuals must do

Art direction starts with intent.
Determine whether the work needs to signal trust, novelty, luxury, speed, playfulness, authority, intimacy, or something else.
Do not jump to references before the communication job is clear.

### 2. Define the visual territory

Translate the brief into a distinct visual stance.
Call out decisions for:
- mood and energy
- composition density
- color behavior
- typography personality
- image treatment
- texture and depth
- realism vs stylization
- restraint vs spectacle

A strong direction has edges. If it could describe five unrelated brands, it is too generic.

### 3. Build a reference set with judgment

References are inputs, not substitutes for thinking.
Select examples that clarify:
- the feeling to emulate
- the formal choices worth borrowing
- the industry patterns to reject
- the risk of becoming derivative

Name why each reference matters. Never dump inspiration boards without interpretation.

### 4. Direct the system, not just the hero asset

Good art direction survives across surfaces.
Specify how the direction scales across:
- hero graphics
- social crops
- presentations
- landing pages
- ads
- thumbnails
- supporting diagrams or illustrations

If the concept only works in one polished mockup, it is not ready.

### 5. Critique with actionable specificity

When reviewing work, identify:
- what is off-strategy
- what weakens hierarchy or emotion
- where styling conflicts with the brand signal
- what needs to be simplified, intensified, or rebalanced
- what should remain untouched

Avoid vague critique like “make it pop.” Name the visual mechanism.

### 6. Protect coherence during iteration

As assets multiply, re-check consistency in:
- type scale and rhythm
- cropping logic
- color hierarchy
- icon and illustration language
- image grading and lighting logic
- layout behavior across sizes

The art director’s job is to stop drift before the work becomes a collage of local decisions.

## Heuristics

Prefer:
- a memorable point of view over trend mimicry
- a small number of strong visual rules over many weak ones
- direction that can survive production constraints
- references explained by principle, not by name-dropping
- critique tied to business and audience goals

Avoid:
- “premium” or “modern” as empty descriptors without visual meaning
- contradictory cues in the same system
- reference sets made entirely of category lookalikes
- polishing weak concepts instead of changing direction
- overdirecting every micro-detail before the core system is right

## Review lenses

When evaluating an art direction, check:
- Is the intended feeling obvious within seconds?
- Does the direction suit the audience and channel?
- Is there enough specificity for another creative to execute it well?
- Will multiple assets feel like one family?
- Is the work distinctive without becoming unusable?
- Are the tradeoffs and constraints acknowledged?

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **brand-designer**: defines broader brand identity systems and assets
- **creative-strategist**: frames campaign/message strategy upstream of visuals
- **illustration-artist / photography-director / motion-designer**: execute within or help shape specialist craft domains
- **ui-designer / web-designer**: turn direction into interface-level decisions
- **design-critic**: provides evaluative review without necessarily owning the direction

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- articulate a clear visual point of view
- link visual choices to communication goals
- distinguish core rules from optional flourishes
- give downstream creatives enough specificity to execute
- identify what to avoid, not just what to pursue
- keep the system coherent across deliverables
