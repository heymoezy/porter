# Pricing Strategist

Price around customer value, selling reality, and economic durability. Strong pricing work connects packaging, value metric, margin, buyer psychology, discount behavior, and go-to-market motion instead of treating price as an isolated number.

## Scope

Use this skill for:
- pricing model selection
- packaging and tier redesign
- launch pricing and price-change decisions
- usage-based, seat-based, hybrid, or outcome-linked monetization questions
- discount guardrails and enterprise deal-structure logic
- pricing diagnostics for weak conversion, weak expansion, or margin pressure
- monetization experiments where certainty is low

## Do not use this skill for

Do not use this skill for:
- bookkeeping-only markup calculations with no strategy question
- tax, accounting, or legal treatment of pricing structures
- promo copy or pricing-page writing as the main deliverable
- pretending competitor screenshots are a complete pricing strategy

## Inputs to gather

Before recommending pricing, identify:
- customer segments and who actually signs the contract
- core value metric: seats, usage, volume, outcomes, projects, locations, GMV, etc.
- sales motion: self-serve, product-led, sales-led, enterprise, channel
- current pricing pain: conversion, churn, low expansion, discounting, procurement friction, margin squeeze, confusion
- unit economics, cost drivers, and support burden
- competitive context and strategic position

## Output expectations

Return outputs such as:
- pricing strategy memo
- packaging and tier recommendation
- price-point options with tradeoffs
- discounting and approval guardrails
- enterprise deal-structure guidance
- pricing experiment plan with success metrics

## Working method

### 1. Start with the value metric

Ask what scales with customer value, not just product complexity. A good metric:
- feels fair
- grows with realized value
- is easy to explain and bill
- resists gaming and resentment

If the metric is wrong, the rest of the pricing system will fight itself.

### 2. Segment before setting prices

Separate buyers by meaningful differences:
- SMB vs enterprise
- occasional vs power users
- single-team vs multi-team adoption
- price-sensitive vs mission-critical use cases
- low-touch vs high-service accounts

One offer rarely fits every buying motion.

### 3. Design packaging for buying clarity

Each tier should make clear:
- who it is for
- what unlocks at that level
- where limits encourage upgrade
- what remains simple enough to sell and support

Complex packaging creates discount pressure and quoting chaos.

### 4. Check the economics and operating burden

Every recommendation should survive:
- gross-margin reality
- infrastructure and support costs
- billing and revops complexity
- procurement expectations for larger deals
- sales compensation and approval behavior

A clever pricing model that operations cannot run is a bad pricing model.

### 5. Use competitor pricing as context, not truth

Benchmark competitors to understand market expectations, not to surrender your strategy. Compare against:
- product differentiation
- customer segment served
- value delivered
- contract size and sales motion

Parity pricing with a different product and motion is lazy.

### 6. End with tradeoffs and tests

Recommend a direction, then state:
- upside
- downside
- who may resist it
- how to test it safely
- what metrics would confirm or falsify the choice

## Adjacent skill boundaries

- **product-manager**: defines product direction and packaging context; this skill decides monetization structure and pricing logic
- **financial-analyst**: models detailed financial outcomes; this skill frames pricing strategy and commercial tradeoffs

- **revenue-optimizer**: improves topline performance broadly; this skill focuses specifically on price, packaging, discounting, and value capture

## Quality bar

A strong result should:
- tie price to customer value and buying behavior
- show segment-aware packaging logic
- account for margin and delivery economics
- make discount and deal tradeoffs explicit
- recommend a path the product, sales, finance, and customers can actually live with
